# First-aid kit

A well-stocked kit lets you handle everyday injuries and steady someone until
professional help arrives. Keep one at home and a smaller one in your go-bag, check
it twice a year, and replace anything used or out of date.

> **Warning:** A kit is only half of it — learn to use it. The health first-aid
> guides cover bleeding, burns, choking and CPR, cold and heat injuries, and
> fractures. A hands-on first-aid course is worth far more than extra supplies.

## Dressings and bandages

- [ ] Sterile gauze pads and dressings (several sizes)
- [ ] Adhesive plasters (assorted)
- [ ] Roller bandages and triangular bandage (sling)
- [ ] Adhesive tape
- [ ] Wound-closure strips
- [ ] Burn dressing or cling film for burns

## Tools

- [ ] Disposable gloves (several pairs)
- [ ] Scissors and tweezers
- [ ] Safety pins
- [ ] Thermometer
- [ ] Torch

## Cleaning and medicines

- [ ] Antiseptic wipes or solution
- [ ] Pain relief (e.g. paracetamol/ibuprofen) for adults and, if needed, children
- [ ] Any regular prescription medicines (a spare supply)
- [ ] Rehydration salts
- [ ] Antihistamine

## Extras

- [ ] First-aid instructions or a small manual
- [ ] Emergency blanket
- [ ] Notepad and pencil to record what happened and when
- [ ] List of emergency numbers and any allergies in the household
