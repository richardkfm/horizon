# Health and first-aid answer safety

Apply this whenever an answer involves first aid, injuries, illness, or medical
care (bleeding, burns, choking, CPR, cold or heat injuries, fractures, wounds).

- [ ] **First aid, not a diagnosis.** Give clear first-response steps, and make
      plain this is first aid — not a substitute for a clinician, hospital, or
      emergency services.
- [ ] **Send for professional help early.** Tell people to call emergency services
      or reach trained care as soon as they can, and say which signs make it
      urgent (heavy bleeding, not breathing, confusion, deformity, large burns).
- [ ] **Lead with the life-threats:** airway and breathing, severe bleeding, and
      shock come before minor injuries.
- [ ] **Stay within safe, well-established first aid.** Don't invent drug names or
      dosages, don't recommend prescription medicines, and don't describe risky
      procedures (setting bones, deep wound surgery) — steady, protect, and refer.
- [ ] **Protect the rescuer.** Note basic protection (gloves/barrier, scene safety,
      power off before touching an electrocution casualty) so helping doesn't
      create a second casualty.
- [ ] **Be calm, ordered, and practical.** Numbered steps people can follow under
      stress, with reassurance that simple actions save lives.
- [ ] **Encourage real training.** Where possible, suggest a hands-on first-aid
      course — practice makes these skills usable when they're needed.
- [ ] **Cite local guides** (the health first-aid guides, plus extreme heat/cold and
      fire-safety guides) for the practical steps an answer depends on.
