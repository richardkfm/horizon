# Insulate a simple shelter

Staying warm is mostly about *keeping the heat you already have*. Before adding
heating, stop the shelter leaking warmth: seal draughts, then insulate the
surfaces that lose the most heat. The cheapest watt is the one you never spend.

> **Risk:** Sealing a shelter tightly without ventilation is dangerous. Any fuel
> burner (wood, gas, paraffin) needs fresh air and a way for fumes to escape —
> carbon monoxide is invisible and deadly. Keep a vent and, ideally, a CO alarm.
> Keep insulation clear of stove pipes and flues to avoid fire.

## Where heat escapes (tackle in this order)

1. **Draughts:** gaps around doors, windows, and the floor move the most air and
   heat for the least effort to fix.
2. **Roof / ceiling:** warm air rises, so an uninsulated roof is a big loss.
3. **Windows:** single glazing loses heat fast; cover at night.
4. **Walls and floor:** larger jobs, but worth it in a long cold season.

## Materials

- Draught-excluder strip, offcuts of cloth, or rolled rags for gaps
- Bulky dry insulation: wool, straw, sheep's wool, rockwool, or rigid foam
- A vapour-side covering (sheeting/board) to keep insulation dry
- Heavy curtains, blankets, or bubble-wrap film for windows
- A rug or layered cardboard/foam for a cold floor

## Procedure

1. **Seal draughts first:** strip around doors and windows; block unused gaps,
   leaving a deliberate vent for any burner.
2. **Insulate the ceiling:** lay or fix dry, lofty material above the living
   space — keep it thick and uncompressed (loft is what insulates).
3. **Treat windows:** heavy curtains or a clear film add a still-air layer; close
   them at dusk to trap the day's heat.
4. **Add wall/floor layers** as time and materials allow, keeping insulation dry
   — wet insulation barely works and rots.
5. **Shrink the heated space:** curtain off one room to warm rather than the whole
   shelter.
