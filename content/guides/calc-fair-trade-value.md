# Work out a fair trade or barter value

When neighbours trade eggs for firewood or an afternoon's mending for a sack
of apples, both sides want the same thing: to walk away feeling the swap was
fair. Fairness isn't a vibe — it's arithmetic anyone can do, agreed out
loud. A little costing keeps good trades from souring good relationships.

> **Note.** Fair trade is a cooperation value as much as a calculation:
> horizon's line is that scarcity and urgency are reasons to help, not
> leverage to price against. The maths below tells you what something
> *costs*; what your group *charges* a neighbour in trouble is a values
> decision (links to sharing resources and managing a group's commons).

## Cost anything in two parts

**Value ≈ materials in + time in.**

1. **Materials:** what went into it, at what it would cost you to replace —
   including the share of things partly used up (a candle's wax, a blade's
   sharpening).
2. **Time:** hours of work × an hourly rate the group treats as standard.
   The simplest fair default is **everyone's hour counts the same**; some
   groups add a margin for skills that took years to learn. Decide this
   openly, once, not trade by trade.

A jar of pickles: vegetables you grew (say the trade value of 1 kg
produce), a spoon of salt, an hour of work → 1 kg produce-equivalent + 1
hour. A repaired chair: scrap wood + 2 hours. Now they're comparable.

## Pick a yardstick everyone knows

Barter needs a common measure the way building needs a common unit. Pick
one ordinary, divisible, widely-held good — a kilogram of a staple (rice,
flour, potatoes) or an hour of ordinary work — and quote values in it.

```ascii
        the trade board, in "kg staple" units:

        1 hour ordinary work.....2      dozen eggs..............1
        1 kg dry beans...........1.5    jar of pickles..........1.5
        day's firewood bundle....3      hour of tool repair.....2
        1 kg honey...............8      loaf of bread...........1

        (agreed at the monthly meeting -- chalk, not stone)
```
*A posted trade board: everything quoted in one yardstick unit, agreed in
the open, revised as seasons change.*

- **Quote both sides in the yardstick, then compare.** Firewood day (3) for
  two dozen eggs (2): close, but the wood-cutter is giving one unit extra —
  either add a loaf, or note the difference and let it ride (see below).
- **Post the going rates** where the group trades. Open numbers protect
  newcomers and quiet people from bad deals, and stop haggling from
  rewarding pushiness (the same reason consensus beats steamrolling).
- **Revise with the seasons:** eggs are cheap in spring, firewood dear in
  autumn. A monthly look at the board keeps it honest — scarcity may move a
  price *some*; hunger shouldn't move it *much*.

## When values don't match

Most real swaps don't balance exactly. Three fair endings:

- **Top up** with something small until both sides agree it's level.
- **Carry the difference:** a notebook of small IOUs ("owes 1 unit") that
  the pair or the group's stock book tracks — half of neighbourly trade is
  running credit, and writing it down keeps it friendly (links to tracking
  a household or group's stores).
- **Round in the other side's favour** and say so. In repeated trades
  between neighbours, alternating generosity is worth more than exactness.

## Check yourself

- **Would you take the other side of this trade?** If not, it isn't fair
  yet.
- **Does the price only work because they're desperate?** Then it's not a
  price, it's leverage — help now, settle later on ordinary terms.
- **Are the same few people always slightly ahead?** Look at the yardstick
  and the board — quiet drift in one direction is how resentment starts
  (links to making fair group decisions).
