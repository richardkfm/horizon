# Support someone through shock, trauma, or grief

Physical injury isn't the only harm a frightening event causes. Shock, fear,
and grief afterward are normal, common, and treatable with ordinary human
care — you don't need to be a counsellor to help someone through the first
hours and days.

> **Note:** This is psychological *first aid* — the calm, human support that
> helps someone get through the immediate aftermath. It isn't therapy, and
> it doesn't replace it. Needing more help later is normal too.

## In the first minutes

- **Get physical needs met first**: safety, warmth, water, a quieter place —
  it's very hard to process anything while cold, thirsty, or still in
  danger.
- **Stay close and stay calm.** Your steady presence often matters more than
  anything you say.
- **Ask what they need** rather than assuming — "what would help right now?"
  beats guessing.

## Listen well

- **Let them talk at their own pace.** Don't press for details of what
  happened; follow their lead.
- **Don't judge or minimise** what they're feeling. Comparisons ("at least
  you...") shut people down rather than helping.
- **Silence is fine.** You don't need to fill every gap with words.

> **Avoid if:** the person doesn't want to recount what happened in detail —
> don't push them to. Forcing someone to relive a traumatic event in detail
> right afterward can do more harm than good; let them lead.

## What to say (and not say)

- Avoid "I know exactly how you feel" — you don't, and it can feel
  dismissive even when well-meant.
- Avoid rushing to reassurance ("it'll be fine") before they've had a chance
  to say how they feel.
- Simple, honest acknowledgement works well: "that sounds really hard" or
  "I'm glad you're safe now."

## Watch for signs that need more than first aid

> **Risk:** Seek professional help urgently if someone can't calm down after
> a reasonable time, can't manage daily basics (eating, sleeping, caring for
> themselves or others) for several days, or talks about harming themselves.
> These need more than first aid can offer.

## Helping children

- **Keep routines as normal as you can** — familiar meals, bedtimes, and
  activities are reassuring even when everything else has changed.
- **Answer questions honestly, at their level**, rather than avoiding the
  topic — children usually sense when something is being hidden.
- **Reassure through presence and action**, not just words — staying close
  says more than explaining does.

## Look after yourself too

Supporting someone through crisis is draining, whether you're a family
member, neighbour, or the person who did the physical first aid. Rest, eat,
and get your own support afterward — a helper who is running on empty is
less able to help the next person who needs it.
