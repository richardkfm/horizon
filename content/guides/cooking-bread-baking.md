# Bake bread without modern equipment

Bread is one of the oldest staple foods, and you can make it with nothing but
flour, water, salt, heat, and time. From a quick flatbread on a hot stone to a
risen sourdough loaf, baking turns stored grain into daily food. Everything here
is **plant-based**.

> **Heat is the variable to master.** Without a thermostat oven, you control the
> bake by managing the fire and using stored heat (a hot stone, a covered pot, an
> earth oven). Practice teaches the rest.

## Start with flatbread (easiest)

Flatbreads need no rising and no oven:

1. Mix **flour, water, and a little salt** into a stiff dough; knead briefly and
   rest it.
2. Divide and **roll or press thin**.
3. Cook on a **hot dry stone, griddle, or pan** over the fire, a minute or two
   each side until blistered.

This alone gives you daily bread anywhere you have flour and fire.

## Grow a sourdough starter

Without bought yeast, wild yeast does the work:

1. Mix equal parts **flour and water** in a jar; leave loosely covered somewhere
   warm.
2. **Feed it** with fresh flour and water once a day, discarding some each time.
3. In several days it bubbles and smells pleasantly sour — it's alive and ready.
   Keep feeding it to keep it going.

## Bake a risen loaf

1. **Mix** flour, water, salt, and a good spoon of active starter into a dough.
2. **Knead** until smooth and stretchy to build structure.
3. **Rise** slowly (hours) in a warm place until well puffed — sourdough is
   patient.
4. **Shape**, let it rise again, then **bake** with strong, steady heat.

## Bake without an oven

- **Covered pot ("Dutch oven"):** bake the loaf in a lidded heavy pot set in or
  over coals — the lid traps heat and steam like a small oven.
- **Earth or clay oven:** build a domed oven, fire it hot, rake out the embers,
  and bake in the stored heat — excellent for bread.
- **Hot stones / ashes:** flatbreads and simple loaves bake on or even buried in
  clean hot ashes.

## Keep it going

Store flour sealed against damp and pests, keep a little starter back to raise the
next batch, and save some grain to mill. With a living starter and a fire, you
never run out of bread.
