# Move people and goods by water

Before roads, heavy things moved by water — and where a river, canal, or lake
connects places you care about, it still beats land transport for weight: a
load that stalls a handcart floats behind one person with a pole. Water
transport is slow, quiet, fuel-free, and dangerous only when treated
casually.

> **Risk.** Water is the serious hazard in this guide, not the boat. Cold
> water kills fit adults in minutes; loads shift; rivers change with every
> rain. Never load a craft so it can't be swum away from, keep every person
> afloat-able (life jacket, or lashed buoyant material with body-securing
> straps as a stopgap), and let someone ashore know the plan. If in doubt,
> don't launch.

> **Pick this if** water already connects your places and loads are heavy or
> bulky. **Avoid if** the water is fast, flooded, or cold and you're new to
> it — start on still water, in warm months, near the shore.

## Start with a raft on still water

A log or barrel raft is the simplest cargo craft: stable, slow, nearly
unsinkable, and buildable from what's around.

```ascii
   barrel raft, from above:              lashing, from the side:

    deck poles lashed across             pole
   =========================            ______
   | (O)    (O)    (O)     |        ___/______\___
   |  barrels lashed under  |        \  X lash  X /
   | (O)    (O)    (O)     |         (  barrel   )
   =========================          \__________/
      ^ sealed, air-filled,
        bung UP and closed
```
*Six sealed barrels under a lashed pole deck: each barrel gives roughly its
volume in litres of lift — keep total load under half the theoretical lift.*

- **Buoyancy first:** sealed barrels or drums (bungs up), or dry, light
  logs. Rule of thumb — a litre of trapped air lifts a kilogram; load to
  **half** that, so the raft floats high even when things go wrong.
- **Lash, don't nail:** square lashings hold flexing timber that would work
  nails loose (links to tying essential knots and lashings).
- **Test empty, then loaded, at the shore** before the first real trip, and
  re-check lashings after every outing.
- Move it with a pole in shallow water, paddles or oars in deeper — and stay
  out of current until the raft and crew have proven themselves.

## Small boats: more skill, more range

A canoe, rowing boat, or flat-bottomed punt carries less than a raft but
goes further, faster, and against gentle current.

- **Flat-bottomed boats** are the cargo workhorses: stable, shallow-draught,
  and the easiest hull to build or repair with plain planks and pitch or
  tar.
- **Load low and centred**, heaviest in the middle, lashed so nothing slides;
  keep generous freeboard (height of the side above water) — an overloaded
  boat is fine until the first wave.
- **Learn to read the water:** a "V" pointing downstream marks the clear
  channel; ripples over smooth water mean shallows; anything uniform and
  glassy above a drop deserves a look from the bank first.
- **Maintain the hull** — dry it out, re-seal seams each season, and fix
  small leaks before they're big ones (the tools-and-repair habit, applied
  to a boat; links to sharpening and repairing hand tools).

## A rope ferry for a regular crossing

Where the same crossing happens daily — fields on the far bank, a neighbour
group across the river — a **fixed rope or cable ferry** beats boating every
trip: stretch a well-anchored line above the water, and pull a raft or punt
across along it. No skill needed per crossing, works loaded, and the line
keeps the craft from being swept downstream. Keep the line high or flagged
so it can't catch other river users, and inspect anchors and rope wear
weekly.

## Plan the water network, not just the craft

- **Map your water:** where it's deep enough, where the shallows, weirs, and
  fast reaches are, seasonal changes, and the portage paths around obstacles
  (links to navigating with a map and compass).
- **Build simple landings** — a firm bank, a post to tie to — at the places
  you actually load and unload; most cargo damage happens at the water's
  edge.
- **Share craft like carts:** a raft or punt serves a whole bank of
  neighbours with a rota and a maintenance habit (links to sharing resources
  and managing a group's commons).
