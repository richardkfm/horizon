# Estimate weights and loads in building

When you build something people stand on, sleep under, or store weight on, the
numbers stop being optional — a floor or roof that fails can kill. This guide
shows how to estimate loads and think about safe spans, and just as importantly,
**when to stop and get expert help.**

> **Safety is not the place to cut corners.** These are estimating methods for
> small, simple structures, with generous margins. For anything large, public,
> multi-storey, or carrying many people, get a qualified engineer. When unsure,
> overbuild.

## Two kinds of load

- **Dead load:** the permanent weight of the structure itself — beams, floor
  boards, roof covering, earth.
- **Live load:** the changing weight it must carry — people, furniture, stored
  goods, snow on a roof, wind.

The structure must safely hold **dead + live load together**, on its worst day.

## Estimate the weight

1. **Work out volumes** of each material (see *Calculate area and volume*).
2. **Multiply by the material's density** (weight per m³ — e.g. water 1000 kg/m³;
   most timber far less; earth, stone, and concrete much more) to get dead load.
3. **Add live load** by area: estimate people and goods, or use a generous
   per-m² figure for the room's use, × the floor area.
4. **Total it** — this is what the floor or roof must carry.

## Spans and supports

- **Shorter spans are far stronger.** A beam's strength drops sharply as it gets
  longer, so add a post or wall to shorten a span rather than hoping a long beam
  holds.
- **Deeper beams are much stronger than wider ones** — depth (height) matters
  most in a joist or beam.
- **Spread the load:** more, closer-spaced joists share weight; wide footings
  stop posts sinking into the ground.
- For specific safe spans, **use proven span tables** for your timber and
  spacing, or copy a known-good local build — don't guess at the edge.

## Build in a safety margin

- **Design for more than you expect.** A safety factor (carrying well above the
  calculated load) covers hidden flaws, weak timber, and overload.
- **Account for the worst case:** a full store, a snow-laden roof, a crowd in one
  room.
- **Watch the supports and connections** — many failures are at joints, not in the
  middle of a beam. Tie things together well.

## Know your limits

Estimating gets you a safe small shed, floor, or shelter. For a home people live
in, a heavy store, or anything others rely on, treat these numbers as a first
check only and confirm with span tables, local building knowledge, or an engineer
before you trust it with lives.
