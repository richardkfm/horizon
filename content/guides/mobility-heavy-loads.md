# Move heavy loads without a car

Almost everything a household needs to move — water, firewood, a harvest, a
sack of grain, a piece of furniture — can move on human power if the load is
on wheels and balanced right. The car isn't what makes heavy loads movable;
leverage and rolling friction are. A neighbourhood that keeps a few good
handcarts and trailers shared and in working order can move as much as it
needs to, sustainably, without fuel.

> **Pick this if** you need to shift weight regularly (water runs, firewood,
> a harvest) without a car. **Avoid if** the load genuinely needs powered
> transport (long distance, very heavy, urgent) — know the limit of
> human-powered hauling rather than injuring yourself proving a point.

## Wheels beat muscle every time

- **A wheel under a load** turns lifting into pushing — the same reason a
  loaded wheelbarrow is easy to move but is exhausting to carry by its
  handles held level with nothing underneath.
- **Bigger wheels roll over rough ground and small obstacles** far more
  easily than small ones; a wheelbarrow with a small hard wheel struggles on
  gravel where a bike-wheeled cart rolls straight over it.
- **Match the vehicle to the load and terrain:**

| Vehicle | Good for | Limits |
|---|---|---|
| Wheelbarrow | Small, awkward, or very heavy single loads over short distances | Needs constant steering and balance; tiring over distance |
| Handcart / sack truck | Stacked boxes, sacks, crates | Needs reasonably flat, solid ground |
| Two-wheeled garden cart | Firewood, harvests, general bulk loads | Slower over long distances than a bike trailer |
| Bike trailer | Heaviest loads over the longest distances, least effort | Needs a sound bike and a rider fit to tow — see *build a cargo bike trailer* |

## Load it right

- **Centre the weight over the axle**, not out over the handles — an
  overhung load fights you on every bump and is what actually causes most
  cart and wheelbarrow tip-overs, not the total weight.
- **Keep the load low.** A high stack is unstable and catches wind; the same
  weight packed low rides steadier.
- **Lash it down** so it can't shift in transit — see *tie essential knots
  and lashings* for lashings that hold under a moving load, not just a static
  one.
- **Balance side to side** as carefully as front to back; an unevenly loaded
  cart pulls to one side on every push.

## Move it without hurting yourself

- **Lift with your legs, not your back:** bend at the knees, keep the load
  close to your body, and straighten your legs to lift — this is true whether
  you're loading a cart or carrying a jerrycan.
- **Push, don't pull, where you can** — pushing lets you use your whole body
  weight and see where you're going; pulling strains the lower back and hides
  what's ahead.
- **Split one big load into two manageable trips** rather than one that
  strains a joint or tips a cart — a load you have to fight is a load that's
  wrong for the vehicle, not a test of strength.
- **Share the load across a team** for anything genuinely heavy (furniture, a
  loaded cart up a slope) — two or three people moving together, calling out
  before each lift, avoid the injuries that come from one person struggling
  alone.

## Keep shared equipment working

A neighbourhood's carts and trailers get more use, and more wear, than any
one household's — treat them accordingly:

- **Keep a simple maintenance routine** — check wheels, axles, and hitches
  before each use, the same way you'd check a bike (see *keep a bicycle
  running with basic maintenance and repairs*).
- **Store dry and under cover** so wood doesn't rot and metal doesn't rust.
- **Agree who's responsible for upkeep** and how borrowing works, the same as
  any other shared resource — see *share resources and manage a group's
  commons* for setting that up fairly rather than letting it default to
  whoever shouts loudest.
