# Treat burns and scalds

Most small burns heal well if you cool them quickly and keep them clean. The single
most useful thing you can do is **get cool running water onto the burn fast** and
keep it there.

> **Do now:** Cool the burn under cool (not ice-cold) running water for at least
> 20 minutes. Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing near the burn before it
> swells — but not anything stuck to the skin.

> **Warning:** Large, deep, or facial burns, burns to a child or older person, and
> any electrical or chemical burn need professional care. This guide is first aid
> only — seek a clinician whenever you can.

## Cool, then cover

1. **Stop the burning** — move away from the heat; smother flames on a person with
   water or by rolling them on the ground.
2. **Cool with water** for 20 minutes or more. Clean cool water is best; if scarce,
   any clean cool liquid is better than nothing. Avoid ice — it can deepen the
   injury.
3. **Keep the person warm overall** while cooling the burn, especially children,
   so they don't get dangerously cold.
4. **Cover loosely** with cling film laid over (not wrapped tight) or a clean,
   non-fluffy cloth. This keeps it clean and eases pain.

## Do not

- **Don't** burst blisters — they protect the skin underneath.
- **Don't** apply butter, oil, toothpaste, or ointments to a fresh burn.
- **Don't** peel away clothing that is stuck to the burn.

## Chemical and electrical burns

- **Chemical:** brush off dry powder, then rinse with lots of running water for at
  least 20 minutes, removing contaminated clothing as you go. Avoid spreading it.
- **Electrical:** make sure the power is off before touching the person. Electrical
  burns can be deep and cause heart problems — get urgent help even if the skin
  looks minor.

## Get urgent help if

The burn is larger than the person's palm, is deep or white/charred, is on the
face, hands, feet, joints, or genitals, or the person is very young, elderly, or
unwell. Watch for signs of shock and keep them warm and still meanwhile.
