# Set up two-way radio for your community

Phone networks and the internet both depend on infrastructure that can fail
— a blackout, a storm, or simple overload can take them down at exactly the
moment a neighbourhood needs to coordinate. Two-way radio doesn't depend on
any of that: it's short-range, licence-appropriate, and useful in ordinary
times too, not just emergencies.

> **Note:** This is about staying in touch with neighbours and coordinating
> mutual aid — not tactical or "secure" communications. Keep transmissions
> plain and treat the airwaves as shared and public, because they are.

## Pick a tier

| Tier | Licence | Typical range | Good for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **FRS / PMR446** | Licence-free | ~1-3 km, less in buildings/hills | Simple handhelds for a household or small group |
| **CB radio** | Licence-free (most countries) | ~3-10 km | Neighbourhood-wide coordination, vehicles |
| **Amateur (ham) radio** | Requires passing a licence exam | 10s-1000s of km depending on band/setup | Longer range, repeaters, a real hobby worth learning |

> **Spec:** Know your local rules before you transmit — licence-free tiers
> have power and antenna limits, and ham bands require a licence tied to
> your call sign. Rules vary by country; check your national regulator.

> **Pick this if:** you want something that works today with no test — start
> with FRS/PMR446 or CB. **Move up to ham radio** if you want real range,
> repeater access, and are willing to study for the licence — it's a
> legitimate, rewarding skill on its own, disruption or not.

## Set up a usable antenna

- **Height matters most.** An antenna a few metres higher clears obstacles
  and roughly doubles useful range — a roof or mast mount beats a handheld's
  built-in antenna every time.
- **Keep it simple.** A basic vertical antenna (a ground-plane or dipole) is
  easy to build and tune, and outperforms a stock antenna significantly.
- **Route feedline carefully:** keep coax away from sharp bends and metal
  that can block the signal, and weatherproof every outdoor connection.

```ascii
        mast, roof, or tall tree
              |
         [ antenna ]   <- height beats power for range
              |
           coax down
              |
         [ radio set ]
```

## Use good etiquette

- **Listen before you transmit** — don't talk over an existing conversation.
- **Keep transmissions short** so the channel stays free for others,
  especially in an actual emergency.
- **Agree a simple channel plan** with your group in advance: a main channel
  for general chat, and a known backup if it's busy or noisy.
- **Use plain, clear language**, not codes or jargon that only some people
  understand — clarity matters more than sounding practised.
- **Test regularly**, not just when you need it — a monthly check-in call
  confirms radios, batteries, and antennas all still work.
