# Triage multiple casualties at once

The calm, one-person priority order in handling common first-aid situations
breaks down the moment several people are hurt at once. Giving your full
attention to the first casualty you reach, while someone a few metres away
has a blocked airway, is the single most common and costly mistake in a
multi-casualty incident. Triage exists to sort everyone fast, so the people
who can still be saved are treated first.

> **Decide:** On your first pass through casualties, you are **sorting, not
> treating** — spend seconds, not minutes, on each person. The only
> treatment worth pausing for on that first pass is opening an airway or
> pressing hard on severe bleeding as you go.

## The sorting method

1. **Call out to everyone who can walk**: "if you can walk, move to
   [a clear point]." Anyone who can get up and walk is your lowest priority
   for now — this single call sorts out a large share of casualties in
   seconds.
2. **Check each person left, in turn: are they breathing?**
   - Not breathing → reposition the airway once (tilt the head back). Still
     not breathing → as hard as this is, move on to the next person. In a
     mass-casualty situation you cannot spend limited time on someone who
     has not responded to one airway attempt while others are savable.
   - Breathing but not responding, or breathing very fast or very slow, or
     bleeding heavily → **highest priority.**
   - Breathing normally and responsive, with lesser injuries → **second
     priority.**
3. **Mark or note each person's priority** as you go — tape, cards, or
   simply calling the priority out loud to any helpers — so nobody is
   treated twice or missed.

```ascii
   Can they walk?  -- yes --> WALKING WOUNDED (lowest priority, sort last)
        | no
        v
   Breathing? -- no --> reposition airway once -- still no --> move on
        | yes                                      (hardest step, but correct)
        v
   Responsive, breathing normally? -- no, or fast/heavy bleeding --> HIGHEST PRIORITY
        | yes
        v
   INJURED BUT STABLE (second priority)
```

## Priority categories

| Priority | Signs | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Immediate** | Not breathing normally after one airway attempt, severe uncontrolled bleeding, unresponsive | Treat and evacuate first |
| **Delayed** | Injured but breathing normally and responsive; can wait a short while | Treat second |
| **Minor** | Walking wounded | Self-care or treat last |
| **Deceased / beyond help** | Not breathing after one airway attempt, in a scene with limited rescuers and multiple casualties | Move on; revisit only if resources allow |

> **Risk:** Treating the first casualty you reach fully, before sorting
> everyone else, is the mistake triage exists to prevent. It feels wrong to
> walk past someone who is hurt — but it is what saves the most lives when
> help is limited.

## Reassess as you go

Triage is not a single pass. Someone marked "delayed" can deteriorate;
recheck breathing, responsiveness, and bleeding in walking wounded and
delayed casualties periodically, especially if help is slow to arrive.

## Working with helpers

If more than one person is helping, split the roles: one person sorts and
calls out priority while others begin treating the immediate-priority
casualties — starting with stopping severe bleeding, which costs only
seconds and saves lives even mid-sort.

## Where to go next

- Learn the single-patient priority order first if you haven't: handling
  common first-aid situations.
- The two most urgent hands-on skills after sorting: stopping severe
  bleeding and splinting fractures and sprains.
- Once physical care is underway, don't forget the people who helped or
  witnessed it: support someone through shock, trauma, or grief.
