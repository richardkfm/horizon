# Maintain and repair computers

Most computer problems come down to a handful of parts that wear out —
storage, memory, and batteries — plus dust and heat. Diagnosing and fixing
these keeps a machine useful for years past when most people would replace
it, and it's the same skill that makes turning an old PC into a local
server possible in the first place.

> **Risk:** Always unplug and remove the battery (or let a built-in battery
> fully discharge) before opening a case. A swollen or leaking battery is
> a fire and chemical hazard — handle it carefully and don't puncture it.

## Diagnose the common problems

| Symptom | Likely cause | Check first |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Won't turn on at all | Power supply, battery, or a loose connection | Try a different power cable/outlet; reseat the battery |
| Turns on, no display | Loose memory or display connection | Reseat RAM; check the display cable if accessible |
| Very slow, especially starting up | A failing or full hard drive | Free up space; consider a storage swap (below) |
| Randomly restarts or shuts down | Overheating or a failing battery | Clean dust from vents/fans; check battery health |
| Battery drains fast or swells | Worn-out battery cell | Replace the battery — never keep using a swollen one |

## Swap the parts that actually fail

**Storage (hard drive or SSD):**

1. Back up anything important first — a failing drive can die completely
   at any time.
2. Remove the old drive (usually a few screws behind an access panel) and
   fit the replacement in the same slot.
3. Reinstall the operating system, or restore from a backup image if you
   made one beforehand.

**Memory (RAM):**

1. Open the memory access panel and note how the existing modules are
   seated (which way round, how they clip in).
2. Release the retaining clips, remove the old module, and press the new
   one in firmly until the clips close on their own.
3. Power on and confirm the system recognises the new amount of memory.

**Battery:**

1. Power off fully and remove external power before starting.
2. Remove the old battery (screws or clips depending on the model) without
   forcing or puncturing it.
3. Fit the replacement the same way it came out, reconnect, and let it
   charge fully before judging its performance.

## Prevent problems before they start

- **Clean dust from vents and fans** every few months — heat is one of the
  biggest causes of early failure.
- **Keep it on a hard, ventilated surface**, not soft bedding that blocks
  airflow underneath.
- **Avoid deep-discharging batteries repeatedly** where you can; partial
  charge cycles are gentler over a battery's lifetime.

## Dispose of dead hardware responsibly

- **Never bin batteries** — they're a fire risk in general waste and
  contain materials worth recovering; take them to a proper collection
  point.
- **Wipe storage drives** before disposing of a whole machine, even a dead
  one, since data can sometimes still be recovered.
- **Salvage before you discard:** working RAM, storage, or a good screen
  from a machine that's otherwise dead can keep another one running — see
  turn an old PC into a local server for one good use of salvaged parts.
