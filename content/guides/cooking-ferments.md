# Ferment beyond pickles: vinegar and lacto-ferments

Fermentation is the oldest food technology that still beats its modern
replacements: no electricity, no canning gear, and the food comes out *more*
nourishing than it went in. The basics of salt-fermenting cabbage are covered
in the preserving guide (links to preserving food by drying, fermenting, and
pickling); this one goes further — brining any vegetable, brewing vinegar
from scraps, and learning to read a jar so you can rescue or reject with
confidence.

> **Note.** Everything here is plant-based and needs only salt, water, jars,
> and time. The one thing fermentation can't fix is spoiled produce — start
> clean and fresh, and the microbes do the rest.

## The brine method: ferment any vegetable

Shredded-and-squeezed works for cabbage; for everything else — whole beans,
carrot sticks, garlic cloves, radishes, chillies — use a poured **brine**.

- **The ratio to memorise: ~1 part salt to 50 parts water by weight** (a 2%
  brine — about one level tablespoon per litre). Saltier (up to 4-5%) ferments
  slower and keeps crunch; use plain salt without additives.
- Pack vegetables into a clean jar, pour brine to cover completely, and
  **weigh them down** — a smaller jar, a boiled stone, a folded cabbage
  leaf. *Submerged = safe* is the whole game.
- **Lid loose, or burp daily.** Fermentation makes gas; a sealed jar
  becomes a slow spring-loaded mess.
- Ferment at cool room temperature. Days 2–5: bubbles and cloudiness —
  that's success, not spoilage. Taste from day 4; when it's pleasantly
  sour, lid on and move it somewhere cold to hold.

```ascii
      day 0        day 2-5         day 7+
     _______       _______        _______
    |brine  |     | o  o  |      |       |
    |~~~~~~~|     |~o~~o~~|      |~~~~~~~|      taste it:
    |veg    |     |veg  o |      |veg    |      sour + crisp
    |pressed|     |cloudy!|      |settled|      = done, move
    |_______|     |_______|      |_______|      to the cold
     salty         alive          sour
```
*A brine ferment's honest timeline: cloudy, bubbling brine mid-week is the
ferment working — clear-to-cloudy is expected, not a fault.*

## Vinegar: the ferment that makes your pickles

Vinegar is a two-step ferment — sugar to alcohol, alcohol to acid — and it
runs happily on scraps you'd compost.

1. **Scrap vinegar:** loosely fill a wide jar with apple peels and cores
   (or bruised fruit), cover with water plus a spoon of sugar per cup, and
   cover with a cloth — air *in*, flies *out*. Stir daily.
2. **Week 1–2:** it froths and smells like cider — the yeasts are making
   alcohol. Strain out the solids once the froth settles.
3. **Weeks 3–6:** the acetic bacteria (from the air and the fruit itself)
   turn the alcohol sharp. A jelly-like mat may form on top — that's a
   **vinegar mother**, a living starter: move it to your next batch and it
   will run faster.
4. **Done when it tastes like vinegar.** Bottle it sealed. Homemade
   strength varies, so for pickling that relies on acidity, taste for a
   clearly sharp vinegar — and keep the pantry rule from the preserving
   guide: when in doubt, refrigerate or eat fresh rather than store.

> **Tip.** One jar of scrap vinegar always going turns fruit waste into the
> household's supply of cleaner, hair rinse, marinade base, and pickling
> liquid — a small loop that never needs the shop (links to composting and
> soil restoration for everything the jar can't take).

## Read the jar: rescue or reject

Fermenting safely is mostly knowing what you're looking at.

- **Normal:** cloudy brine, bubbles, sediment, a yeasty-sour smell, white
  film that wipes off (kahm yeast — harmless; skim it, push everything
  back under the brine).
- **Reject:** fuzzy mould in colours (green, black, pink), a rotten —
  not sour — smell, or slimy, mushy vegetables. Don't salvage a fuzzy
  ferment by scooping; the jar's contents go to the compost and the jar
  gets a hot wash.
- **Most failures trace to one cause:** something poked above the brine.
  Weights and topped-up brine prevent nearly everything.

> **Risk.** Trust your nose and eyes over any timetable — sour and clean
> means fermenting; foul means finished. If a jar smells rotten or shows
> coloured fuzz, discard it without tasting.

## A small fermenting habit

- **Stagger small jars weekly** instead of one huge crock: a rolling supply,
  and one failed jar costs little.
- **Label with date and salt ratio** — future-you can't remember, and the
  label is how you learn which ratio suits your kitchen's temperature.
- **Sour is a pantry ingredient:** brine livens soups and dressings
  (unheated, it keeps its live cultures), vinegar preserves the next glut,
  and a spoon of active brine kick-starts a sluggish new jar.
