# Lead group singing

Singing together costs nothing, needs no equipment, and does what little else
can: it steadies a frightened group, makes hard work lighter, and turns strangers
into a community. Anyone can lead it — you do not need a "good voice".

> **Everyone belongs in the song.** Pitch it so the shy and the tone-deaf can
> join. The point is togetherness, not performance.

## Choose songs that teach themselves

The easiest group songs need no sheets and no rehearsal:

- **Call and response:** you sing a line, the group sings it back (or a fixed
  reply). New people learn by copying, line by line.
- **Rounds:** everyone sings the same short tune, but groups start at different
  times so the parts overlap. Simple yet rich.
- **Repeating choruses:** one verse leader, a chorus everyone already knows.

## Lead it step by step

1. **Set the pitch low and gentle** so most voices can reach it. Hum the first
   note for everyone.
2. **Teach by ear:** sing one short phrase, have the group echo it, then add the
   next. Don't move on until it's solid.
3. **Keep the beat** with a clap, stamp, or a drum so the group stays together.
4. **Use your hands** to show louder/softer and start/stop, so words aren't
   needed mid-song.
5. **Add parts gradually:** split into two groups for a round only once the tune
   is comfortable.

## Build a shared repertoire

- Collect songs the group already knows — work songs, lullabies, local tunes.
- Write down or memorise the words so they aren't lost (see the storytelling
  guide).
- Make new songs for new moments: a planting song, a song that names the people
  who keep the water clean. Songs carry memory.
