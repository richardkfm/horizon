# Build a cargo bike trailer for hauling loads

A cargo trailer turns an ordinary bicycle into a load-hauling vehicle — water
jerrycans, firewood, a harvest, a sack of grain, tools — all moved on human
power alone. This is the practical, buildable relative of the classic Dutch
**bakfiets** (a bike with the load box built into the frame itself, ahead of
the rider): a bakfiets needs a purpose-built or welded frame, which is a real
metalworking project. A **trailer** gets you most of the capacity with wood,
salvaged wheels, and simple joinery, and it hitches to whatever bike you
already have.

> **Spec:** aim for a trailer that carries **40-60 kg** on flat ground behind
> an unmodified bike. Beyond that, braking and control suffer unless the bike
> and rider are set up for it — several smaller loads on repeat trips beat one
> trailer overloaded past control.

## Plan before you build

- **Match the axle track to your riding routes** — narrow enough for gates,
  paths, and doorways; wide enough to stay stable. 50-70 cm between wheels is
  a workable range for most household use.
- **Salvage the wheels first**, then design the frame around them — a
  secondhand bike wheel (with its axle) is the single hardest part to
  fabricate from scratch, so let it drive your dimensions rather than forcing
  a found wheel to fit a fixed plan.
- **Keep the load low and centred over the axle.** A high, rear-heavy load
  makes the trailer fishtail and lifts weight off the tow bike's rear wheel,
  which is exactly the load-bearing wheel you need braking and traction from.

## Build the frame and axle

1. **Build a rectangular base frame** from timber (or welded box-steel, if
   you have the tools) sized to your salvaged wheels and intended box.
2. **Mount the axle** across the frame, either reusing the bike wheel's own
   axle in simple frame-mounted dropouts (easiest — no bearings to source) or
   running both wheels on one continuous axle through pillow-block bearings.
3. **Brace the corners** diagonally so the frame can't rack sideways under a
   swaying load — the same principle as bracing a timber-frame wall.

```ascii
        [box, plywood sides]
       /-------------------\
      |                     |
       \-------------------/
         |                |
        (O)==== axle ====(O)      wheels either side, axle centred
              |
           [hitch] -----> tow bar back to the bike's seatpost/rear axle
```

## Fit a hitch

The hitch is what makes or breaks a trailer — it needs to pivot in more than
one direction so the trailer follows the bike through turns and over uneven
ground without transmitting every bump straight to the frame:

- **A simple ball-and-socket or U-bolt hitch** at the bike's rear axle or
  seatpost, with a pin or bolt that lets the tow arm swing both
  side-to-side and up-and-down, works for most loads.
- **Use a locking pin or a safety strap** as backup in case the primary hitch
  ever works loose — a trailer coming loose in traffic is dangerous, not just
  inconvenient.
- **Lash the tow arm to the frame** with a secure lashing (see *tie essential
  knots and lashings*) if you're building from timber and don't yet have a
  welded or bolted joint — check and retighten it before each heavy trip
  until you're confident in it.

## Build the box

- **Plywood or slatted timber sides**, screwed or bolted to the base frame,
  sized to what you'll actually carry (water containers, firewood lengths,
  harvest crates).
- **A drop-down or removable tailgate** makes loading heavy items far easier
  than lifting them over a high side.
- **Tie-down points** (eye bolts or loops of cord) at the corners so loads can
  be lashed down rather than shifting loose — see *tie essential knots and
  lashings* for lashings that hold under a moving load.

## Make it safe to ride

- **Fit a reflector or light** at the rear, and reflective tape on the sides —
  a trailer widens your profile in traffic and needs to be seen.
- **Test-ride unloaded first**, then with a light load, before trusting it
  with a full one — check the hitch holds, the trailer tracks straight, and
  braking distance with a load feels controllable.
- **Check bolts and the hitch pin** before every heavy trip; a trailer shakes
  fasteners loose faster than the bike itself.
