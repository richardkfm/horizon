# Cook with foraged and wild food

Wild greens, roots, nuts, and fruit were everyday food for most of human
history, and they still grow free at the edge of every path. This guide picks
up where identification ends: you're holding a plant you are *certain* about,
and the question is how to make it worth eating — because most wild food needs
a cook's help to shine.

> **Risk.** Identification comes first and is its own discipline — never
> cook anything you can't name with total confidence, and learn the deadly
> lookalikes before the recipes (links to identifying safe wild food and
> avoiding poisoning). When in doubt, leave it out: no meal is worth the
> gamble. Skip wild mushrooms entirely unless someone locally expert
> teaches you specific species in person.

## Gather like the patch matters

Wild food only stays food if the plant outlives the harvest.

- **Take a share, not the stand:** a common rule of thumb is no more than
  one plant in three from a patch — and less for anything scarce. Leave the
  best plants to seed.
- **Prefer leaves and fruit over roots** — picking leaves is a haircut;
  digging the root is the whole plant. Save root harvests for abundant,
  fast-spreading species.
- **Pick clean ground.** Avoid roadsides, sprayed field margins, dog-walked
  verges, and flood-washed banks; wash everything anyway.
- **Rotate your spots** through the season so no single patch carries you.

## The forager's kitchen: four moves

Most wild plants are stronger-flavoured, tougher, or more bitter than their
garden cousins. Four techniques tame nearly all of them:

```ascii
     WASH ---> BLANCH ---> (LEACH) ---> COOK
      |          |            |           |
    grit,     wilts        soaks out    soup, fry,
    insects,  toughness,   bitterness   pie, dry
    dust      kills sting  & tannins    for later
```
*The pipeline: everything gets washed; most greens get blanched; only the
bitter and tannic need leaching; then cook as you would any vegetable.*

1. **Wash** in plenty of water, twice for anything low-growing.
2. **Blanch** — a minute in boiling water — to wilt tough greens, mellow
   strong ones, and neutralise nettle sting. Squeeze and treat like
   spinach.
3. **Leach** the genuinely bitter or tannic: repeated soaks or boils in
   fresh water until a nibble tastes mild. This is the acorn trick below.
4. **Cook** as normal: wild greens into one-pot stews, fritters, and pies
   (links to cooking simple one-pot plant-based meals); roots roasted or
   boiled; fruit into compotes or dried for winter (links to preserving
   food by drying, fermenting, and pickling).

## Three worked examples

- **Nettles** — the gateway wild food. Pick young tops with gloves in
  spring; blanch one minute (sting gone), then cook as spinach: nettle
  soup, nettle-and-grain bakes. Rich in iron and protein, and cutting the
  tops thickens the patch.
- **Acorns** — abundant calories under nearly every oak, but tannic. Shell,
  chop, then leach: soak in fresh water, changing it when it browns, until
  a piece tastes bland instead of drying-bitter (hours to days depending on
  the oak). Dry and grind into meal for flatbreads and porridge — worth it
  in a mast year when they carpet the ground.
- **Wild fruit and hips** — often mouth-puckering raw, transformed by heat
  and patience: cook to a compote, dry into leathers, or steep rosehips
  (halved, itchy seeds strained out) into a syrup that carries vitamin C
  through winter.

> **Tip.** A new-to-you plant, even a safe one, earns a small first
> serving — any food can disagree with someone, and wild ones are strong.
> Introduce one new species at a time so you know what caused what.

## Fold it into ordinary cooking

Foraging isn't a survival stunt — it's a seasonal habit that stretches the
garden and the budget in perfectly stable times.

- **Treat wild food as the seasoning, not the whole plate**: a handful of
  wild greens in a garden soup, wild fruit over a grain porridge. Familiar
  dishes with wild additions get eaten; all-wild experiments often don't.
- **Follow the calendar:** spring greens, summer fruit, autumn nuts and
  hips. A one-page note of what you found, where, and when becomes a
  personal harvest map that improves every year.
- **Dry or preserve gluts** the same day you pick them — wild harvests come
  in bursts, and an afternoon's drying turns a burst into a shelf.
