# Diagnose crop problems — deficiency, disease, pest, or invasive

A struggling plant can mean several very different things, and the wrong
response wastes effort or makes it worse — feeding a diseased plant more
compost does nothing, and spraying a nutrient-starved one for pests does
nothing either. Work through the pattern methodically before you act.

> **Decide:** Look at *how the problem is spread* before looking at the leaf
> itself. An even problem across a whole bed points to soil or water; a
> problem on scattered single plants points to pest or disease; a problem
> that keeps spreading day by day is actively contagious and needs isolating
> now.

## Read the pattern first

| What you see | Likely cause |
| --- | --- |
| Every plant in a bed or field evenly affected | Soil, nutrient, or water issue |
| A few scattered individual plants affected, rest fine | Localised pest or disease |
| Symptom spreading outward from one point over days | Active disease or pest, spreading |
| Same problem, same spot, every year | Soil deficiency, drainage, or a soil-borne disease |

## Nutrient deficiencies

Deficiencies tend to be even across a bed and symmetric on the plant. The key
question is **which leaves** — nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium move
within the plant, so the plant robs its own older leaves first; the
micronutrients below do not move, so they show up in new growth first.

| Nutrient | Where it shows | What you see | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Nitrogen (N) | Older, lower leaves | Even pale yellowing, weak spindly growth | Compost, manure, plant a nitrogen-fixer nearby |
| Phosphorus (P) | Older leaves, stems | Dark green or purple tinge, stunted, poor roots | Bone meal, compost, avoid cold wet soil |
| Potassium (K) | Leaf edges | Scorched brown edges, weak stems, poor fruit/flower | Wood ash (sparingly), compost |
| Iron / manganese | New growth | Yellowing *between* veins, veins stay green | Improve drainage, correct overly alkaline soil |
| Magnesium | Older leaves | Yellowing between veins, sometimes reddish tinge | Compost, occasional Epsom-salt drench |
| Calcium | Fruit and growing tips | Blossom-end rot on fruit, dying growing tips | Even, consistent watering; lime if soil is very acidic |

> **Spec:** If the yellowing is even, symmetric, and affects most plants in
> the bed the same way, treat it as a deficiency first — not a disease —
> and correct the soil before you do anything else.

## Disease signs

Diseases are usually patchy, one-sided, or spreading, and often follow wet,
still, or crowded conditions.

- **Leaf spots** (circular, often with a ring or dark edge) — usually fungal;
  remove and destroy affected leaves, improve airflow by spacing or pruning.
- **Powdery or downy coating** on leaves — mildew; thrives in still, humid
  air; space plants further apart and water the soil, not the leaves.
- **Sudden wilting** of a whole plant despite moist soil — often a
  soil-borne wilt or root rot; pull and destroy the plant, don't replant the
  same crop there next season.
- **Soft, dark, spreading rot** on leaves or stems, especially in wet
  weather — blight; remove affected material immediately, before it spreads
  to neighbouring plants.

> **Risk:** Do not compost diseased plant material — many fungal spores and
> wilts survive composting and come right back with the compost. Burn it or
> bin it away from growing beds.

## Pest damage signs

- **Ragged holes or chewed leaf edges** — chewing insects or slugs; hand-pick
  in the early morning or evening, or use a barrier (crushed shell, copper
  tape) for slugs.
- **Curled, sticky, or distorted new growth** — sap-sucking insects (aphids,
  etc.); a strong water spray, encouraging predator insects, or a soap
  spray usually controls them.
- **Pale winding trails inside a leaf** — leaf miners; remove and destroy the
  affected leaves before the larvae mature.
- **Holes or tunnels in roots or tubers when harvested** — soil-dwelling
  grubs; rotate crops next season rather than replanting the same family in
  that spot.

> **Do now:** If a problem is visibly spreading day to day — wilting,
> soft rot, or a pest population exploding — remove and isolate the affected
> plants immediately rather than waiting to be sure of the exact cause. A
> day's delay can cost the whole bed.

## Invasive and overly aggressive plants

Not every fast grower is welcome. An invasive spreads beyond where you
planted it, out-competes your food crops and local wild plants, and rarely
has a local pest or predator keeping it in check.

- **Recognise it:** spreads well beyond its planted bed on its own; sets seed
  or sends out roots and runners faster than you can use or remove it; keeps
  coming back after you've cleared it.
- **Contain it:** cut it back before it sets seed, dig out spreading roots
  rather than just the top growth, and keep any known spreader in a
  contained bed or large buried pot rather than open ground.
- **Never compost seed heads or roots of a spreading plant** — dispose of
  them separately (deep burial or burning), or you will replant the problem
  with your own compost.
- **Check before you plant an unfamiliar fast grower**, especially one
  offered as a quick windbreak, cover crop, or reforestation pioneer — see
  the reforestation guide for choosing species that won't escape.

> **Tip:** Walk your growing area's margins every few weeks specifically
> looking for anything spreading past where you put it. Catching a spreader
> in its first season is a five-minute job; catching it after two seasons
> can be a season's worth of digging.

## Prevent the next problem

- **Rotate crop families** bed to bed each season so soil-borne pests and
  diseases don't build up in one spot.
- **Space and prune for airflow** — most fungal disease needs still, damp
  air to take hold.
- **Mix crops rather than planting a single variety** across a whole plot —
  the same principle as choosing crops: one failure becomes a setback, not a
  famine.
