# Make and keep a fire without matches

Fire lets you boil water safe to drink, cook food, stay warm, dry clothes, and
signal for help. The skill is mostly preparation: a fire that "won't light" is
almost always one that was rushed.

> **Safety first:** Clear a circle of bare earth, keep water or soil within
> reach, never leave a fire unattended, and don't light one in high wind or near
> dry brush. Put it out cold — stir the ashes, douse, and feel for heat.

## Gather three sizes of fuel first

Collect everything *before* you try to light it:

1. **Tinder** — dry, fluffy material that catches a spark: birch bark, dead grass,
   cattail fluff, char cloth, fine wood shavings, cotton lint.
2. **Kindling** — dry twigs from pencil-thin up to finger-thick. Snap them; if
   they bend instead of snapping, they're too damp.
3. **Fuel wood** — thumb-thick and larger, added once the fire is established.

Dead branches still attached to a tree or standing wood are usually drier than
wood off the ground.

## Lay the fire

- **Teepee:** stand kindling in a cone over a tinder bundle. Lights fast, good
  for boiling.
- **Lean-to:** rest kindling against a thicker log over the tinder; the log
  blocks wind.

Leave gaps so air can flow — fire needs fuel, heat, *and* air.

## Get an ember

- **Spark (ferro rod or flint and steel):** scrape sparks straight into a tight
  tinder bundle, then blow gently until it flames.
- **Friction (bow drill):** spin a hardwood spindle against a softwood board to
  grind a small dark dust pile; keep going until it smokes and forms a glowing
  ember, then tip it into your tinder bundle and blow.
- **Sun:** focus sunlight with a lens (glasses, a clear water-filled bag) onto
  the tinder until it smoulders.

## Build it up and keep it

- Feed the new flame the smallest kindling first, then steadily larger pieces.
- Keep a dry reserve of tinder and kindling under cover for the next light.
- To carry fire, bank it: cover hot coals with ash so they smoulder for hours.
