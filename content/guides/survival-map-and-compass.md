# Navigate with a map and compass

A map and compass together answer two questions a phone can't once it's out
of battery or signal: **which way should I go**, and **where am I right now**.
This assumes you already have both a paper map of your area and a compass —
see find direction without a compass if you have neither.

> **Before you move:** Tell someone your plan, or leave a marker. A map and
> compass reduce the *chance* you get lost; they don't replace telling someone
> where you're headed.

## Orient the map

Before reading anything off a map, turn it so it matches the ground:

- Lay the compass flat on the map with its long edge running along a north-south
  grid line.
- Rotate the map and compass together until the compass needle lines up with
  the map's north arrow.
- The map is now "oriented" — a hill drawn to your left on the map is a hill to
  your left on the ground.

## Take and follow a bearing

A bearing is a direction given in degrees (0-360°), read clockwise from north.

- **From the map:** lay the compass edge between where you are and where you
  want to go, then rotate the compass's dial until its lines match the map's
  north-south grid lines. The number at the direction-of-travel arrow is your
  bearing.
- **Follow it on the ground:** hold the compass flat, turn your whole body
  until the needle sits inside the dial's marked outline, and walk in the
  direction the arrow points. Pick a landmark on that line, walk to it, then
  recheck the bearing — this is far more accurate than trying to walk a
  straight line by eye over a long distance.

```ascii
              N (0/360)
               |
      315 \    |    / 45
           \   |   /
   270 -----  YOU  ----- 90
           /   |   \
      225 /    |    \ 135
               |
              S (180)

  bearing = angle from north, measured clockwise, to your destination
```

> **Tip:** A phone compass app or a old-fashioned watch can back up a
> baseplate compass, but a simple magnetic compass keeps working with no
> battery at all — worth having one in a go-bag for that reason alone.

## Find your position (resection)

If you know roughly where you are but want to confirm it, or you're properly
lost but can see two or more identifiable landmarks (a peak, a tower, a
junction):

1. Take a bearing **from your position to** each landmark with the compass.
2. Convert each to a **back bearing** (add 180° if the bearing is under 180°,
   subtract 180° if it's over) — this is the direction from the landmark back
   to you.
3. On the map, draw a line from each landmark along its back bearing. Where
   the lines cross is your position.

Two landmarks are enough in theory; a third catches mistakes, since all three
lines should meet at (roughly) the same point.
