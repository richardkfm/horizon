# Choose and set up a safe campsite

Where and how you camp matters more than what gear you carry. A good site
keeps you dry, warm, safe from falling branches and flash floods, and out of
reach of animals looking for an easy meal.

> **Decide:** Choose your site with at least an hour of daylight left. Sites
> that look fine in daylight can hide hazards, and a rushed choice in the
> dark is how people end up on a flood path or under a dead branch.

## Read the ground before you commit

| Avoid | Why |
| --- | --- |
| Dead standing trees or large dead branches overhead ("widowmakers") | Can fall without warning, especially in wind |
| Low ground, dry stream beds, valley floors | Flash floods and cold air pool here |
| A single tall tree, or a ridge top, in open ground | Highest lightning risk in a storm |
| Animal trails, dense brush, or fresh droppings | You're camping in another animal's path |
| Anthills, wasp nests, or standing water nearby | Stings and biting insects |

| Look for | Why |
| --- | --- |
| Slightly raised, level, well-drained ground | Rain runs away from you, not under you |
| A natural windbreak (rise, rock, tree line) upwind | Cuts wind chill overnight |
| Within sight or earshot of water, but not right next to it | Convenient without flooding you or crowding wildlife's only water source |
| Morning sun exposure | Warms and dries the camp early in the day |

## Lay out the camp

Keep sleeping, cooking, food storage, and toilet areas well separated —
mixing them is how food attracts animals to where you sleep, and how a
latrine ends up too close to your water.

```ascii
                        upwind / upslope
                              ^
                    water source (do not camp on the bank)
                              |
              -----------------------------------
             |                                     |
      food storage                            sleeping area
      (60m+ downwind,                          (central, sheltered,
       hung or in a                             away from dead trees)
       sealed container)                              |
             |                                        |
              -----------------\        /-------------
                                 \      /
                             cooking / fire area
                             (downwind of tents, clear of
                              overhanging branches)
                                    |
                                    v
                        latrine (60m+ from water AND
                          downhill/downwind of camp)
```

## Store food away from wildlife

- **Never keep food, wrappers, or anything scented in your tent** — that
  includes toothpaste and soap. It is the single biggest reason animals come
  into camp.
- **Hang food** in a bag at least 4 m up and 2 m out from the trunk of a tree,
  or use a sealed, animal-resistant container if hanging isn't possible.
- **Keep the cooking area and food storage well away from where you sleep** —
  a hungry animal following a food smell should never have to pass your tent
  to reach it.
- **Clean up food scraps and wash-up water fully** before dark; don't leave
  dishes to soak overnight.

> **Risk:** A dead standing tree or a heavy dead branch overhead can fall
> with no warning, wind or no wind. Look up before you pitch, not after.

## Leave the site as you found it

- **Use an existing site** if one exists rather than clearing a new one.
- **Keep any fire small** and in an existing fire ring; scorched, bare ground
  takes years to recover.
- **Pack out everything you packed in**, including food scraps — burying
  rubbish just teaches animals to dig.
- **Bury or manage human waste properly** rather than leaving it — see the
  sanitation guide.
