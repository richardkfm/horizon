# Reforest and restore woodland

A single windbreak hedge helps one plot. A few hectares of returning woodland
does more: it steadies the water table, stops erosion at a landscape scale,
and eventually supplies fuel, timber, food, and shelter for wildlife and
people alike. It is slow work — measured in years, not seasons — but most of
it is patience and protection rather than labour.

> **Decide:** Before planting a single tree, find out whether the land can
> regrow on its own. Where a seed source and stumps or roots survive nearby,
> **protecting** existing regrowth from grazing and fire is faster, cheaper,
> and hardier than planting from scratch.

## Read the site first

- **Stumps, root suckers, or seedlings already coming up** — the land wants
  to reforest itself; your job is protection, not planting.
- **Grazing animals or foot traffic keep browsing new growth down** — this is
  usually the real reason bare ground stays bare, more often than a lack of
  seed.
- **No trees within seed-dispersal distance and no stumps to resprout** — you
  will need to plant nursery stock or direct-sow seed.
- **Exposed, wind-scoured, or eroding ground** — start with the earthworks and
  cover techniques in the soil and land restoration guide; young trees do not
  survive on ground that cannot hold water yet.

## Natural regeneration first

Assisted natural regeneration (ANR) works with what is already trying to grow
back, and usually beats planting on both cost and survival rate:

1. **Fence or hedge off the area** from livestock and heavy foot traffic —
   browsing is the single biggest killer of young regrowth.
2. **Mark and protect existing stumps, suckers, and seedlings** rather than
   clearing them; a cut stump of many species resprouts from its own
   established root system, far faster than a new seedling can grow.
3. **Cut back only the toughest competing weeds and grasses** directly around
   protected seedlings so they get light; leave the rest as ground cover.
4. **Control fire risk** — a single burn can undo years of regrowth.

> **Pick this if:** you see any seedlings, suckers, or resprouting stumps
> already present — fence the area and protect what's there before you
> spend any effort planting. It is almost always faster.

## If you need to plant

Where no seed source or stumps remain, plant a **mixed** stand, not a single
species:

| Role | What it does | Examples (match to local natives) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pioneer / nitrogen-fixer | Grows fast in poor, exposed soil; fixes nitrogen for others | Alder, acacia, black locust, sea buckthorn |
| Nurse | Shelters slower canopy species while they establish, thinned out later | Fast-growing local pioneers, willow, birch |
| Canopy / timber | The long-term forest structure; slow but long-lived | Oak, chestnut, pine, local hardwoods |
| Food / fodder | Feeds people and animals as the woodland matures | Fruit and nut trees, mulberry, hazel |
| Understory | Fills the lower layers once canopy is establishing | Native shrubs, berries, shade-tolerant natives |

> **Spec:** Always use species **native to your region**, or already
> naturalised and non-invasive there. A tree that thrives *too* well outside
> its native range can escape and out-compete everything else nearby — see
> the invasive-species section of the plant-diagnosis guide before choosing
> an unfamiliar fast grower.

understory shrubs filling the gaps — the pioneers shelter and feed the
canopy trees while they establish, then get thinned for firewood as the
canopy closes over.*

1. **Plant in the dormant season** (bare-root stock establishes far more
   cheaply than potted stock, and survives better if planted while dormant).
2. **Mulch each tree** generously to hold moisture and suppress competing
   grass — grass competition kills more young trees than drought does.
3. **Guard every sapling** against browsing animals (a simple stake-and-mesh
   guard) until it is above browsing height.
4. **Water through the first dry season** if you can; after that, a
   well-mulched, well-chosen native tree should not need irrigation.

> **Risk:** Planting a single fast species across the whole area is the same
> mistake as a monocrop field — one pest, disease, or hard winter can wipe
> out the entire planting. Mix species and ages.

## Manage the young woodland

- **Weed around saplings** for the first two or three seasons — after that,
  their own shade suppresses most competition.
- **Thin overcrowded stems** as the canopy closes; thinnings become firewood,
  poles, or mulch, and thinning lets the remaining trees grow stronger.
- **Coppice suitable species** (cut at the base every several years for
  regrowth) for a repeatable, sustainable pole and firewood supply that never
  needs replanting.
- **Never clear-cut** a young or recovering woodland — selective, staggered
  harvest keeps continuous cover, keeps the soil protected, and keeps the
  woodland regenerating itself.

## A first plan

1. **Survey for existing regrowth** — stumps, suckers, seedlings — and fence
   or protect it first.
2. **Fix water and erosion** on any bare or exposed ground before planting
   (see soil and land restoration).
3. **Plant a mixed, native stand** only where nothing is regrowing on its
   own, following the pioneer/canopy/understory mix above.
4. **Guard and mulch** every planted tree through its first two seasons.
5. **Thin and coppice** as the stand matures, taking wood without ever
   clearing it bare.

## Where to go next

- If the ground is bare, compacted, or eroding, start with soil and land
  restoration — reforestation depends on it.
- Choosing a fast-growing but wrong species risks becoming invasive; check
  the plant-diagnosis guide's invasive-species section first.
- Fruit and nut trees can double as both canopy/food-layer species and a
  dedicated orchard — see planting fruit trees and berry bushes.
