# Spin fibre and weave simple cloth

Making cloth is worth doing alone, disaster or not — it's one of the oldest
useful skills there is. This guide covers *fine* fibre work: spinning thread
for cloth. For *thick* cord and rope for tying and hauling, see make rope and
cordage from plant fibre — the reverse-wrap method there is a different,
coarser technique from the spinning covered here.

> **Tip:** Start with wool if you can get it — it's more forgiving to spin
> than plant fibre while you're learning the motion.

## Prepare the fibre

**Plant fibre (flax, nettle, hemp):**

1. **Ret the stems:** lay cut stalks in a shaded damp spot (or soak them in
   water) for one to three weeks until the woody core has softened and
   partly rotted away from the fibre — check every few days; over-retting
   weakens the fibre.
2. **Break and scutch:** once dry again, crush the stalks to break the woody
   core, then scrape it away, leaving long fibre strands behind.
3. **Comb (hackle)** the fibre through closely-spaced pins or a stiff brush
   to separate and align the strands, removing the last bits of woody debris.

**Wool:**

1. **Skirt and clean** the fleece — remove heavily soiled or matted edges.
2. **Card it:** brush small amounts between two hand-cards (or dog-grooming
   brushes) until the fibres lie roughly parallel in a loose roll (a "rolag").

## Spin thread with a drop spindle

A drop spindle — a simple weighted stick — turns prepared fibre into
continuous thread.

```ascii
       twisted thread
             |
             v
        ,---------,
        | spindle |   <- weighted disc (whorl) partway down the shaft
        '----+----'
             |
          [ hook ]     thread wraps here as it winds on
```

*Fig. 1: a drop spindle — the weight keeps it spinning while gravity pulls it
downward, twisting the fibre fed in from above into thread*

1. **Attach a short starter thread** to the spindle with a few wraps and a
   hitch through the hook.
2. **Draft fibre** from your prepared bundle with one hand while the other
   sets the spindle spinning (roll it down your thigh, or flick it with your
   fingers).
3. **Let the twist run up** into the drafted fibre as the spindle drops,
   turning loose fibre into thread.
4. **Wind on** the finished length onto the spindle shaft once it's spun,
   then reattach at the hook and repeat.
5. **Ply for strength (optional):** spin two full spindles of thread, then
   spin them together in the opposite direction to lock them into a
   stronger, balanced yarn.

## Weave a basic cloth

A simple frame loom is enough to start:

1. **Build a frame** (four sticks lashed or nailed into a rectangle) and
   string it with strong **warp** threads, evenly spaced, top to bottom.
2. **Weave the weft** over-and-under across the warp with a shuttle (a flat
   stick with thread wound on), alternating which threads it passes over
   each row.
3. **Beat each row down** firmly with a comb or flat stick so the weave
   stays even and tight.
4. **Finish the edges:** knot off the last rows so the weave can't unravel
   once removed from the frame.
