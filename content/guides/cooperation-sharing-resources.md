# Share resources and manage a group's commons

A group that pools tools, land, food, or labour can do far more than any one
household — but only if the system for sharing is seen as fair. Goodwill runs
out fast the moment someone feels they give more than they get, or that
someone else is taking without contributing. This guide covers the ongoing
system; see the goods-to-share-and-barter checklist for what to stock.

> **Principle:** A commons survives on being visibly fair, not on trust alone.
> Make contribution and access easy to see, so fairness doesn't depend on
> everyone's memory or goodwill matching up.

## Decide what's pooled and what isn't

- **Be explicit.** List what's shared (a tool shed, a water source, a plot of
  land, a rota of labour) and what stays personal — ambiguity is where
  resentment starts.
- **Match the system to the resource.** A shared water source needs simple
  turn-taking; shared land might need a rota; shared tools need a
  borrow-and-return log.

## Rotate labour fairly

- **Share the unpleasant jobs**, not just the visible or enjoyable ones —
  rotate cleaning, night watch, or repetitive tasks the same as anything
  else.
- **Weigh contribution by capacity, not a flat split.** Someone caring for
  young children or recovering from illness contributes differently — agree
  this openly rather than silently expecting the same from everyone.
- **Track it lightly.** A visible rota or tally (a shared sheet, a chalkboard)
  settles "who did the last one" disputes before they start.

```ascii
   week 1        week 2        week 3        week 4
  [ A: water ]  [ B: water ]  [ C: water ]  [ A: water ]
  [ B: watch ]  [ C: watch ]  [ A: watch ]  [ B: watch ]
  [ C: clean ]  [ A: clean ]  [ B: clean ]  [ C: clean ]

   rotation shifts by one each week — everyone takes every job in turn
```

*Fig. 1: a simple rotating rota — three people, three jobs, one shift each
week so no one job or person is fixed in place*

## Allocate fairly, not just equally

- **Need first, then contribution.** In real hardship, weight access toward
  who needs it most; in steady times, contribution and equal shares are
  usually simplest.
- **Keep a visible, simple record** of what's been drawn from a shared store
  — not to police people, but so no one has to wonder or ask.
- **Revisit the split openly** if circumstances change (a new household
  joins, a source runs low) rather than letting an old arrangement quietly
  become unfair.

> **Risk:** the most common failure isn't dishonesty — it's a fair split
> becoming unfair as circumstances change and nobody says so. Build in a
> regular, low-drama moment to ask "does this still feel fair?"

## Handle free riders without punishment first

- **Assume good faith initially:** a missed shift is often a misunderstanding
  or a real hardship, not exploitation — ask before accusing.
- **Make the pattern visible, not personal:** "the water rota has missed
  three weeks" invites a group fix; "you never show up" invites defensiveness.
- **Escalate through governance, not gossip,** if a pattern continues — see
  the governance guide's recall process for roles, and the resolving-conflict
  guide for one-to-one disputes.
