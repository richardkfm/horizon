# Set up fair, accountable governance for a group

Making one good decision together (see the group-decisions guide) is
different from building a structure that stays fair over months and years.
Governance is that structure: who holds what power, for how long, and what
stops it from concentrating in one place.

> **Principle:** No role should be permanent, unaccountable, or unremovable.
> The moment a position stops being answerable to the people it affects, the
> group has stopped self-governing — whatever it calls itself.

## Why structure matters, not just goodwill

A group that relies purely on everyone being reasonable will eventually meet
someone who isn't — or will simply drift, as the person with the most time,
confidence, or information quietly ends up deciding everything. Structure
exists to make fairness hold up even when people, moods, and circumstances
change.

> **Risk:** The most common failure isn't a hostile takeover — it's a
> well-meaning founder or organiser who never rotates out, until the group's
> memory, contacts, and habits all live in one person no one can question.

## Spread power on purpose

- **Rotate roles.** Give any position with real authority (treasurer,
  facilitator, spokesperson) a fixed term and a successor lined up before it
  ends. Rotation is the single strongest defence against quiet capture.
- **Split power that shouldn't sit together.** Whoever proposes a spend
  shouldn't be the only one who approves it; whoever runs meetings shouldn't
  also be the only one who sets the agenda.
- **Make recall real, not theoretical.** Agree in advance, while everyone is
  calm, on a simple way to remove someone from a role — a clear trigger and a
  known process, not an unwritten assumption that it "shouldn't be needed".

```ascii
        proposes ----> reviews ----> approves ----> records
        (member)        (rotating    (whole         (rotating
                         panel)       group vote)     secretary)

   no single seat controls more than one stage of the chain
```

*Fig. 1: splitting a decision into stages held by different, rotating people
— no one seat controls the whole chain from proposal to record*

## Choose a decision model that fits your group's size

| Group size | A workable model | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Under ~15 | Whole-group consent (see group-decisions guide) | Everyone can be heard directly |
| ~15-50 | Small rotating councils per area (food, water, safety…), reporting back to the whole group | Keeps detail work moving without every decision needing everyone |
| 50+ or several sites | Federate: each local group governs itself and sends a rotating, recallable delegate to a shared council for only the decisions that cross groups | Keeps power local; the shared council can't outgrow its mandate |

> **Pick this if:** your group is growing past whole-group meetings — federate
> before you centralise. A shared council of delegates from self-governing
> local groups keeps decisions close to the people they affect, and any
> delegate who stops representing their group can be recalled by it.

## Keep it transparent

- **Write decisions down** and keep them somewhere anyone in the group can
  read — a hidden decision is one that can't be checked or questioned.
- **Share resources and access openly:** who holds keys, funds, tools, or
  contacts, and how someone new gets access to them.
- **Review the structure itself** on a set schedule (each season, each new
  member) — governance that can't be questioned or changed is exactly the
  kind that calcifies into unaccountable power.

## Guard against the return of hierarchy

- Watch for informal titles hardening into permanent authority ("the
  founder", "the expert") even without a formal vote.
- Actively bring in quieter or newer voices for rotating roles, not just
  whoever already has confidence or free time.
- Treat "we don't have time for process" as a warning sign, not a reason to
  skip it — it is usually the moment power is being taken, not saved.
