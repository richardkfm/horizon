# Save and store seeds for next season

Saving your own seed makes a garden self-sustaining: free plants every year,
varieties adapted to *your* soil and climate over time, and independence from
buying seed. It is one of the oldest and most important food skills.

> **Save from open-pollinated plants.** Seed from "F1 hybrid" plants won't grow
> true to the parent. Choose open-pollinated or heirloom varieties for reliable
> saved seed.

## Choose the right plants

- **Save from your best:** the healthiest, most productive, best-tasting plants —
  this slowly improves your stock.
- **Save from several plants**, not just one, to keep the variety vigorous.
- **Let the crop fully mature** — often past the eating stage. Lettuce must bolt
  and flower; beans must dry on the plant; squash must over-ripen.

## Easy crops to start with

- **Dry-seeded (easiest):** beans, peas, lettuce, grains. Let pods/heads dry on
  the plant, then collect.
- **Wet-seeded:** tomato, cucumber, squash, melon. Scoop seeds from ripe fruit,
  rinse off the pulp (tomatoes benefit from a few days' ferment in water), and
  dry.

Avoid crossing: keep different varieties of the same crop apart, or grow only one
variety of each, so seed stays true.

## Clean and dry

1. **Remove** seeds from pods, husks, or fruit.
2. **Rinse** wet seeds and spread them on a plate or cloth.
3. **Dry thoroughly** in a warm, airy, shaded place — damp seed rots or sprouts in
   storage. A seed should snap, not bend.

## Store and label

- Keep dry seed in **airtight containers** (jars, tins, sealed packets) somewhere
  **cool, dark, and dry**.
- **Label everything** with the variety and the year — it's impossible to tell
  later.
- Add a little dry charcoal or ash to absorb moisture if you have it.

## Keep the cycle going

Test old seed by sprouting a few on a damp cloth before planting season. Share
and swap seed with neighbours — a community seed store is resilience against any
single bad year.
