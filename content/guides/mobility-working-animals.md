# Move people and goods with a working animal

Where loads outgrow handcarts and bike trailers — a season's firewood, water
for many households, timber, a whole harvest — a working animal has been the
sustainable answer for thousands of years. A donkey, mule, horse, or ox runs
on grass and hay, fertilises the land it works, and hauls several times what
a person can, day after day.

> **Note.** horizon covers working animals as *partners* — carrying, pulling,
> and riding — never as food. An animal that works for you depends on you;
> taking one on means committing to its feed, water, shelter, and care for
> its whole working life, not just the busy season.

> **Pick this if** your group regularly moves loads beyond human-powered
> range and can genuinely commit to daily animal care. **Avoid if** loads are
> occasional and cart-sized — a shared handcart or cargo trailer is far less
> responsibility (links to moving heavy loads and building a cargo trailer).

## Choose the right animal for the work

- **Donkey:** the beginner's working animal — hardy, long-lived, thrifty on
  rough forage, carries packs (up to roughly a quarter of its body weight)
  and pulls a small cart. Calm but needs patient, fair handling.
- **Mule:** a donkey's toughness with more size and strength; excellent packs
  and draught, but needs experienced handling.
- **Horse:** fast and strong in front of a cart, but costlier to feed and
  more delicate than a donkey.
- **Ox (trained steer):** slow, immensely strong, and steady — the classic
  draught animal for ploughs and heavy wagons; runs entirely on grazing.
- Whatever the species, **buy or borrow with an experienced person present**,
  and prefer a trained, middle-aged animal over a young untrained one.

## Load and harness so the animal lasts

An animal is not a machine: a bad load or ill-fitting harness injures it in
one season and ends its working life in a few.

```ascii
   pack saddle: weight split evenly, riding on padded bars,
   NEVER pressing on the spine:

              load  A   load          spine stays free:
              ____  |   ____
             |    | | |    |               A
             | 50 |_|_| 50 |            ___|___
             |____|     |___|          |   .   |   <- gap over backbone
              /   padded   \           | _/ \_ |
             /  bars either \          pads sit on
                side of spine          muscle, not bone
```
*A pack load: split into equal halves, carried on padded bars either side of
the backbone — the spine itself carries nothing.*

- **Balance every load side to side** — a lopsided pack sores the back in
  hours. Weigh or heft both sides before loading.
- **Keep total pack weight to about a quarter of body weight**, less on
  steep ground, in heat, or for old or unfit animals. Carts multiply what an
  animal can move — pulling on wheels beats carrying.
- **Padding everywhere harness meets the body**, with no rubbing seams;
  check for warm spots and sores at every unloading, and rest any animal
  that shows them.
- **Fit harness to *this* animal** and re-check the fit as it gains or loses
  condition through the year.

## Daily care is the real cost

- **Water twice daily and feed matched to work** — grazing plus hay covers
  light work; hard daily hauling needs supplementary feed.
- **Feet first:** most lameness is in the hooves. Pick them out daily, learn
  basic trimming or find who can, and rest the animal on soft ground.
- **Shelter from rain, wind, and sun**, dry standing, and company — equines
  especially suffer when kept alone.
- **Work in the cool hours**, build fitness gradually, and give at least one
  full rest day in seven, more in heat or heavy seasons.

## Handle with patience, not force

- Animals work willingly for handlers they trust. Move calmly, speak before
  you touch, and use the same simple commands every time.
- **Never punish fear.** A balky animal is usually hurting, overloaded, or
  frightened — find the cause instead of forcing it.
- Teach children and newcomers the safe zones (never directly behind, watch
  the feet) before they help.

> **Tip.** One working animal can serve several households. Sharing one —
> with a clear rota for its work, feed, and care — spreads the commitment
> and keeps the animal busy enough to stay fit (links to sharing resources
> and managing a group's commons).
