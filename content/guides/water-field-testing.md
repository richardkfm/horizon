# Test whether water is safe to drink

You rarely need a lab to make a sensible first judgement about water. Field
observation plus a few cheap tests tell you most of what you need to decide
*how* to treat a source before drinking from it.

> **Risk:** No field test rules out every contaminant. Clear, odourless water can
> still carry pathogens, nitrates, or heavy metals. Treat unfamiliar water as
> unsafe until you have both tested it *and* applied a treatment step. When in
> doubt, boil.

## What to look at first

- **Source and surroundings:** is there farmland, industry, mining, latrines, or
  livestock upstream? These hint at chemical or faecal contamination that no
  filter fully removes.
- **Clarity (turbidity):** cloudy water shields pathogens from disinfection.
  Always reduce turbidity (settling, cloth, sand filter) before treating.
- **Smell and colour:** rotten-egg, fuel, or chemical smells, or unusual colours,
  mean *do not drink* — these point to problems treatment may not fix.

## Simple checks

- **Turbidity tube / jar test:** fill a clear jar; if you cannot read text
  through it, pre-filter before disinfecting.
- **pH and hardness strips:** cheap aquarium or pool strips give a rough read;
  extreme pH suggests industrial influence.
- **Nitrate strips:** important where there is heavy fertiliser or sewage use —
  unsafe for infants even when the water looks clean.
- **Faecal indicator tests:** low-cost hydrogen-sulphide (H₂S) test vials darken
  in the presence of bacteria associated with sewage. A positive result means
  disinfect, no exceptions.

## Deciding what to do

1. **Visibly dirty →** settle and filter first (see the slow sand filter guide).
2. **Possible pathogens →** disinfect: boil for one minute (longer at altitude),
   or use chlorine or solar (SODIS) disinfection.
3. **Chemical smell, colour, or known industrial source →** find another source;
   treatment you can do in the field will not make it safe.
4. **Unsure →** treat as unsafe, boil, and retest when you can.
