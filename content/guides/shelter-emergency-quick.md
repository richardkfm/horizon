# Build a quick emergency shelter

When you have no roof, shelter is often a more urgent priority than food or even
water — exposure to cold or wet can become dangerous within hours. A good
emergency shelter is small, dry, and insulated, and you can build one with no
tools but your hands.

> **Small is warm.** A shelter just big enough for your body traps your own heat.
> A large shelter is hard to warm and wastes effort. Build for survival, not
> comfort.

## Pick the spot

- **Dry and sheltered:** out of wind, off wet or low ground that collects water,
  and away from hazards (dead branches, flood channels, rockfall).
- **Near materials and water**, but not *in* a gully or on a ridge top.
- Use what's there: a rock overhang, a fallen tree, a dense thicket, or a wall
  cuts your work in half.

## Quick shelter types

- **Lean-to:** prop a long ridge pole between two supports, lean branches along
  one side, and pile debris over them. Fast; good with a fire in front.
- **Debris hut:** lean a ridge pole from the ground to a low support, rib it with
  branches on both sides, and pile a thick layer of leaves, grass, or bracken all
  over. The best one-person cold-weather shelter without tools.
- **Tarp/salvage shelter:** if you have a sheet, tarp, or plastic, string it as a
  roof and weigh the edges; far faster than natural materials.

## Make it warm and dry

1. **Insulate the roof thickly** — an arm's depth of dry debris sheds rain and
   holds heat. Thin walls do little.
2. **Insulate underneath** — cold ground steals heat fast. Pile a thick bed of dry
   leaves, grass, or boughs to lie on.
3. **Keep the entrance small** and face it away from the wind; block it with a
   pack or debris once inside.
4. **Shed water** by sloping the roof and overlapping materials top-down like
   tiles.

## Then improve it

Once you're out of immediate danger, add a fire reflector, deepen the bedding,
and reinforce the roof. For anything longer-term, move to a sturdier build (see
the earth-building and timber-cabin guides).
