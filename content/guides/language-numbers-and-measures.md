# Learn numbers, measurements, and quantities

After greetings, numbers are the most useful words you can share with someone.
Counting, trading, agreeing a time, and measuring a length all run on the same
small vocabulary — and unlike most words, numbers can always be checked by
showing fingers, objects, or written digits.

> **Tip.** Written digits (1, 2, 3…) are understood almost everywhere, even
> where nothing else is shared. When in doubt, write the number down or scratch
> it in the dirt.

## Start with one to ten

1. **Learn 1-10 first** in the other language, and teach yours. Ten words
   unlock most daily counting.
2. **Then learn the pattern, not more words.** Most languages build bigger
   numbers from small ones (ten-and-one, two-tens). Ask "eleven? twelve?
   twenty?" and listen for the pattern instead of memorising every number.
3. **Add "how many?" and "how much?"** — the two questions that put numbers to
   work.
4. **Check with fingers or objects** while you say a number, until you trust
   each other's words.

> **Note.** Finger counting itself differs between cultures — some start
> counting on the thumb, some on the index finger, and a raised hand can mean
> five, ten, or "stop". Show the count *and* say it, and confirm the total by
> laying out objects when a trade matters.

## Words that make quantities useful

Group these the way you'd use them:

- **Trade:** how much?, more, less, enough, all, half, each.
- **Time:** today, tomorrow, morning, evening, day, hour, "when?".
- **Order:** first, next, last.
- **Comparison:** big, small, long, short, heavy, light, full, empty.

## Measure with what every body shares

When you have no shared units, measure with the body and with containers you
can both see — then agree names for them.

```ascii
   hand span            forearm (elbow to fingertips)      pace
  <--------->          <--------------------------->   <---------->

   \..___../                ________________              O
    \|   |/                |                |            /|\   step
     |   |                 |________________|            / \  ..___..
     thumb tip                                          heel to heel
     to little
     finger tip
```
*Body units everyone carries: a span, a forearm, a pace — agree on one person's
as the standard for a job.*

- **Length:** a hand span, a forearm, a pace. For anything that must fit,
  cut a stick or knot a cord to the exact length and pass *that* around
  instead of a number.
- **Volume:** "this bottle", "this bucket" — hold it up, name it together.
- **Weight:** compare in the two hands, or against a known filled container.
- **Counting stock:** tally in groups of five — four strokes and a bar — a
  system readable across most cultures.

> **Risk.** Mixed units cause real losses in trade and building. When exactness
> matters — medicine doses, water treatment, rationing — use one written system
> (metric where known), write it down, and have both sides repeat the quantity
> back.

## Practise where it counts

- Count real things together: baskets, tools, people at a meal.
- Practise a pretend trade with stones before a real one with goods.
- Put numbers on your shared word list with the digits beside each word
  (links to learning core phrases and to communicating across a language
  barrier).
