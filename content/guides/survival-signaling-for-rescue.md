# Signal for rescue if lost or stranded

If you're lost, injured, or stranded and have shelter, water, and people who
know roughly where you are, the safest choice is usually to **stay put and
make yourself easy to find** — searchers cover ground faster than you can,
and a stationary target is far easier to locate than a moving one.

> **Decide:** Moving on makes sense only if you know exactly where safety is
> and can travel there safely. Otherwise, stop, get comfortable and visible,
> and put your energy into signaling rather than walking further.

## The universal rule: groups of three

Three of anything, evenly spaced, is recognised worldwide as a distress
signal — three fires in a triangle, three blasts on a whistle, three flashes
of light, three piles of rocks. One or two of something reads as normal
background noise; three reads as deliberate.

## Ground-to-air signals

Searching aircraft or drones look for large, high-contrast shapes in open
ground, not people. Build signals as big as you can — each letter or symbol
at least 3 m across — from anything that contrasts with the ground: rocks,
logs, cut branches, spare clothing, or a scraped or trampled patch in snow or
sand.

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **V** | Need assistance |
| **X** | Need medical assistance |
| A large **arrow** | Direction you have gone / are heading |

> **Spec:** Build signals in the most open ground you have — a clearing,
> a ridge, a sandbar, a field — never under tree cover where nothing can be
> seen from above.

## Fire and smoke

- **Three fires** in a triangle or line, each visible from the air and from
  a distance, is a recognised distress pattern.
- **By day**, add green leaves, damp grass, or a little rubber or oil to a
  hot fire to make thick, dark smoke that stands out against sky or
  greenery.
- **By night**, keep fires bright and clear-burning with dry wood — light
  travels further than smoke in the dark.
- Keep a signal fire ready to light at a moment's notice (dry tinder,
  covered from rain) whenever you hear or see a potential searcher.

> **Risk:** Clear the ground around a signal fire and keep it under control —
> in dry conditions an unattended signal fire can start a wildfire that
> makes your situation far worse. See making and keeping a fire safely.

## Mirror or flash signals

A mirror, phone screen, or any shiny metal can be seen for many kilometres in
good sun:

1. Hold the mirror close to your eye, facing roughly toward the target
   (aircraft, a distant ridge, a road).
2. Stretch your other arm out and make a **V** with two fingers, framing the
   target between them.
3. Angle the mirror until the spot of reflected light lands on your fingers —
   that same light is now hitting the target.
4. Sweep it slowly across the target a few times rather than holding it
   still.

## Whistle and sound signals

- **Three sharp blasts**, pause, repeat — a whistle carries much further
  than shouting and costs far less energy.
- Sound travels best in still air and at dawn or dusk; pause and listen
  between signals for a reply.

## Before you go, make finding you easier

> **Tip:** Tell someone your route and expected return time before you set
> out. The single biggest factor in how fast a search succeeds is how
> quickly and precisely people know where to start looking.
