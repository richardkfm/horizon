# Teach simple group dances

Dancing together is one of the oldest ways people celebrate, mourn, and bond.
Circle and line dances are ideal: there are no partners to leave anyone out, the
steps repeat, and people learn just by following the person beside them.

> **Inclusion first:** Offer a way for everyone to take part — seated clapping,
> simpler steps, or just holding the circle. No one should feel left out.

## Why circles and lines work

- **No partner needed**, so numbers never have to be even and nobody is excluded.
- **Repeating steps** mean newcomers catch on within a minute or two.
- **Holding hands or shoulders** creates a literal sense of connection.

## Teach a simple circle dance

1. Form a circle, everyone holding hands or resting hands on shoulders.
2. Teach the footwork **without music first**, slowly, calling each step:
   e.g. "step left, close, step left, touch" — repeated all the way round.
3. Practise the pattern a few times until the circle moves as one.
4. **Add the beat** — a drum, claps, or a song — and start slow.
5. Speed up only when the group is ready; let the energy build naturally.

## Keep it going

- Call the steps aloud as you dance so latecomers can join any time.
- Place confident dancers around the circle so others can follow them.
- Vary it: change direction, add a clap, raise joined hands, or speed up for a
  joyful finish.

## Make it your own

- Adapt steps to who is present — gentler for elders and small children.
- Attach dances to occasions (a harvest dance, a welcome dance) so they gather
  meaning and get remembered and passed on.
