# Use offline maps and GPS with no signal

A phone's GPS receiver finds your position from satellites alone — it needs
no cellular signal, no data plan, and no internet connection. What *does*
need a connection is fetching the map picture around that position. Prepare
this while you still have signal, and both keep working after you lose it.

> **Note:** "GPS" here means the *receiver* in your phone, always working
> passively for free. It's different from your maps *app*, which normally
> streams map images over the internet and stops doing that with no signal —
> unless you've told it to save the area first.

## Before you lose signal

- **Download your area for offline use.** Most map apps, including many
  phones' built-in maps, let you download a region while connected so it's
  stored on the device — search the app's settings for "offline maps" or
  "download area" and cover home, work, and anywhere you might need to
  evacuate to or from.
- **Save specific places, not just the map.** Star or bookmark your home,
  a meeting point, a hospital, and a safe space — searching for an address
  later needs a connection even if the map itself is downloaded.
- **Check this horizon node for map data.** If whoever runs this node has
  installed and rendered an offline map pack (**Admin → Content packs**),
  you can browse it right in the browser under **Maps** — no other source of
  internet needed. Ask them what's available.

## After signal is gone

- Open the maps app as normal — the blue dot showing your live position keeps
  working from GPS satellites alone, with no bars and no wifi.
- Only the **downloaded** area will render; panning outside it usually shows
  a blank grid, since there's nothing left to fetch.
- Turning on airplane mode (while leaving GPS/location on, if your phone
  allows it separately) saves battery without losing your position fix — a
  real concern once charging isn't guaranteed.

## Keep a paper backup

A downloaded digital map depends on a charged phone. A printed paper map of
your area and evacuation routes needs no battery at all and is worth keeping
in a go-bag alongside a compass — see **navigate with a map and compass** for
how to use it. Print it, or ask whoever runs this horizon node to, before you
need it: mid-emergency is the wrong time to discover the printer needs power
too.
