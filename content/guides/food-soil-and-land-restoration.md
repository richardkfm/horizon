# Restore worn-out land and rebuild soil

Most available ground has been stripped, compacted, or eroded rather than
freshly cleared. Before it can feed you well, it usually needs rebuilding:
more life in the soil, more cover on its surface, and better-held water. None
of this needs machinery — it needs time, patience, and a few repeatable
techniques.

> **Principle:** Never leave soil bare. Bare ground loses water to
> evaporation, erodes in rain and wind, and starves the soil life that
> everything else depends on. Cover it with a living plant, a mulch, or both.

## Read the land first

- **Bare, capped, or cracking soil** — surface life has died back; it sheds
  rainwater instead of absorbing it.
- **Water runs off fast, or gullies form** — the land needs to slow and spread
  water before it can soak in.
- **Little grows without heavy input** — the biology that feeds plants
  (fungi, bacteria, worms) has been depleted, not just the nutrients.
- **Bare slopes or wind-scoured ground** — erosion is actively removing what
  little topsoil remains; stop the loss before adding anything back.

> **Decide:** Fix water and cover first, feed the soil second, plant your goal
> crop third. Planting straight into dead, bare, fast-draining ground wastes
> the seed.

## Slow the water down

On any slope, water that runs off is water (and soil) you lose.

```ascii
   uphill                                              downhill
   ~~~~~~\                                            /~~~~~~
          \___________swale (level ditch)____________/
                  |  mound of dug-out soil, planted  |
                  v                                   v
            water soaks in here instead of running off downhill
```

*Fig. 1: a swale — a level ditch dug along the contour, with the dug-out soil
mounded just downhill of it and planted — catches runoff and lets it soak in
instead of carrying topsoil away*

1. **Mark the contour** (a level line across the slope) with a simple line
   level or water-filled hose; water only stops running if the ditch is level.
2. **Dig a shallow ditch** along that line and mound the soil on the downhill
   side.
3. **Plant the mound** with deep-rooted trees, shrubs, or perennials — their
   roots hold the loosened soil and keep drawing the stored water down.
4. On a smaller scale, low stone or log lines across a slope do the same job
   for a single bed or path.

> **Risk:** Never dig a ditch that isn't level — an off-contour ditch
> concentrates water into a fast channel and can cause worse erosion than
> doing nothing.

## Cover and feed the soil

- **Cover crops / green manure:** sow a fast, dense crop (clover, vetch,
  buckwheat, rye) purely to cover bare ground, then cut and leave it, or dig
  it in, before it seeds. Roots open the soil; the cut top feeds it.
- **Mulch everything bare:** straw, leaves, woodchip, or cut cover crop laid
  thick stops evaporation, blocks weeds, and rots down into the soil.
- **Compost what you can:** kitchen and garden waste, manure, and leaves
  turned into compost is the fastest way to reintroduce soil life.
- **Avoid bare tillage:** turning soil over repeatedly burns off the organic
  matter you're trying to build. Disturb the surface as little as possible
  once cover is established.

## Bring back trees and edges

- **Windbreaks:** a line of trees or dense shrubs across the prevailing wind
  shelters crops behind it, cuts evaporation, and stops topsoil blowing away.
- **Mixed edges, not monocrops:** a hedge or tree line of several species
  (nitrogen-fixers like alder or acacia alongside fruit or timber trees)
  recovers faster and supports more of the insects and birds that keep pests
  in check.
- **Let some margins go wild:** an uncultivated strip left to regrow brings
  back pollinators and predators that benefit the whole plot, not just
  itself.

> **Pick this if:** you only have time or energy for one thing this season —
> plant a fast-growing nitrogen-fixing tree or shrub line along your main
> exposed edge. It shelters everything behind it while it also rebuilds soil.

## A first-season plan

1. **Stop the losses:** mark contours and dig swales or check-dams on any
   slope that sheds water.
2. **Cover every bare patch** with a sown cover crop or mulch before the next
   rain or dry spell.
3. **Plant a windbreak/hedge line** along the most exposed edge.
4. **Start a compost heap** from whatever waste you already have.
5. Only once cover and water are holding, **plant your goal crops** (see the
   crop-choosing guide) into the improving ground.

Rebuilding land is gradual — expect visible change over a few seasons, not
overnight. Each season's cover crop and mulch layer leaves the ground a
little better than it found it.
