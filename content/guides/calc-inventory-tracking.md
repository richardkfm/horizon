# Track a household or group's stores and supplies

Stores you don't track quietly run out — usually at the worst moment — or
quietly spoil while you buy more of the wrong thing. A stock book fixes both
with nothing but paper, a pencil, and a few minutes a week, and it's the
arithmetic backbone under any shared store a group runs together.

> **Tip.** Track the few things that matter, not everything you own. Staple
> food, water, fuel, medicines, seed, and any shared materials cover most of
> what can hurt you by running out.

## Set up the stock book

One page per item (or per shelf), three habits:

```ascii
   item: RICE (dry, kg)          minimum: 10 kg
   ------------------------------------------------
   date     | in   | out  | left | note
   ------------------------------------------------
   3 Apr    | 25   |      | 25   | harvest share
   10 Apr   |      | 2    | 23   | week's cooking
   17 Apr   |      | 2    | 21   |
   24 Apr   |      | 3    | 18   | guests
   ------------------------------------------------
                            ^ running total: one
                              subtraction per line
```
*A stock page: what came in, what went out, what's left — and a minimum
level that tells you when to act.*

1. **Write every in and out** as it happens, or once a week from memory
   while it's fresh. The running "left" column is one addition or
   subtraction per line.
2. **Set a minimum level** for each item — the amount that gives you time
   to grow, gather, or trade more before it's gone. When "left" touches the
   minimum, that's your signal, not the empty shelf.
3. **Count the real shelf now and then** (monthly for staples) and correct
   the book to match. Books drift; the shelf is the truth.

## The two numbers that matter

- **Use rate:** out ÷ time. From the page above: 7 kg in 3 weeks ≈ **2.3 kg
  a week**.
- **Days remaining:** left ÷ daily use. 18 kg ÷ 0.33 kg a day ≈ **54
  days**. Do this little division for your staples monthly and you'll never
  be surprised.

Rates also catch problems early: a water barrel emptying faster than people
are drinking means a leak or an unrecorded user — the book notices before
you do.

## First in, first out

Date what you store, shelve new stock **behind** old, and take from the
front. Most "we had plenty" losses are old stock spoiling unseen at the back
(links to preserving food and to keeping a food store — the food-store
checklist pairs with this book).

## For a shared store

- **One book, kept at the store**, and everyone who takes writes — a shared
  store without a book breeds suspicion even among friends.
- **Record who took what** for shared tools and materials (a "borrowed"
  column with a name does it), so things come back.
- **Review together** at a regular meeting: what's below minimum, what's
  not moving, what the group should grow or trade for next (links to
  sharing resources and managing a group's commons, and to working out a
  fair trade).
