# Survive being stranded in a vehicle

A vehicle is usually the safest shelter you have if you're stranded — it's
warmer and drier than the open, it's far easier for searchers to spot than a
person on foot, and it carries tools and supplies with you. The classic
mistake is leaving it to go looking for help on foot.

> **Decide:** Stay with the vehicle unless you know exactly how far help is
> and can travel there safely. A car is bigger, easier to find, and gives
> you shelter and signal options that walking away throws away.

## Prepare before a long trip

Keep a simple kit in the vehicle year-round: a blanket or sleeping bag, warm
clothes, water, some food, a torch, a brightly coloured cloth or flag, a
small shovel, a phone charger or power bank, and a basic first-aid kit. Tell
someone your planned route and expected arrival time before you set off.

## If you're stranded

1. **Make yourself visible.**
   - Use hazard lights briefly, not continuously, to save the battery.
   - Raise the bonnet/hood as a widely recognised distress signal.
   - Tie a brightly coloured cloth to the aerial, mirror, or a door handle.
   - At night, use the interior light sparingly — enough to be seen, not
     enough to drain the battery.
2. **Stay warm, safely.**
   - Run the engine only in short bursts to warm the cabin, and only once
     you've checked the exhaust pipe is completely clear of snow, mud, or
     debris.
   - Crack a window slightly whenever the engine is running.
   - Huddle together for warmth, and insulate yourself from the cold seat or
     floor with anything soft you have — blankets, clothing, seat covers.
3. **Conserve what you have.**
   - Sip water rather than rationing it to nothing; melt clean snow if you
     have fuel to run the engine and no other water.
   - Keep the phone off or in low-power mode, checking for signal only
     occasionally.
4. **Signal for rescue** using the same tools you'd use if lost on foot —
   mirror flashes, a whistle, or ground signals visible from the air; see
   signal for rescue if lost or stranded.

> **Risk:** Never run the engine with the exhaust pipe blocked by snow or
> mud. Carbon monoxide builds up in an enclosed vehicle and can kill without
> any warning smell or warning signs — the same danger as running a
> generator indoors during a blackout.
