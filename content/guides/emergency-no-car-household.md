# Get through emergencies with no car to fall back on

Most emergency advice quietly assumes a car: trunk space for a week of
supplies, the ability to drive to a cooling centre or a relative's spare room,
enough room at home to store a deep pantry. A car-free household — often an
apartment in a city, sometimes by choice, sometimes not — faces the same
floods, blackouts, and heatwaves with a narrower set of moves. Nothing here
replaces the other emergency guides; it fills in the parts that change when
"just drive" isn't an option.

> **Note:** the risk here isn't poverty or ability — plenty of car-free
> households are neither poor nor frail. It's that evacuation, stockpiling,
> and rescue all lean on a vehicle by default, so a walking-and-transit
> household needs to plan those parts on purpose instead of assuming them.

## Plan your evacuation on foot, before you need it

- **Walk at least two routes out**, in different directions, from where you
  live. A flooded underpass, a closed bridge, or a shut train station can
  block the "obvious" way out — you want a second option already in your
  legs, not on a map you're reading for the first time mid-emergency. See
  **use offline maps and GPS with no signal** for getting those routes onto
  your phone (and a paper backup) before you need them.
- **Know your transit's failure mode.** Does the metro flood in heavy rain?
  Do trains stop running above a certain temperature? Does the bus network
  shut down in a blackout? If transit is likely to fail *during* the kind of
  emergency you're planning for, treat it as something that might trap you,
  not rescue you, and have a walking fallback.
- **Scout a walkable safe space on each route** — a library, a shopping
  centre, a municipal building — before the emergency, not during it. For the
  frail or unwell, the walk there is itself a risk; deciding in advance where
  to go turns a stressful search into a short, known trip.
- **Keep a go-bag sized to carry, not to load into a trunk.** One bag per
  person, light enough to walk several kilometres with: water, a torch, any
  medication, copies of documents, cash, a phone charger. Keep it packed near
  the door.

## Store supplies in the space you actually have

Generic advice to keep a week of water and food per person assumes a garage
or a full pantry. In a smaller home, the same reserve has to go vertical and
compact instead: stacked bottles under a bed, a shelf bracket above a door
frame, jars on top of cupboards. See the water and food guides for how much
to store and how to keep it safe over time — the volumes don't shrink just
because the space did, so plan the storage first and fit the quantity to it,
not the other way round.

## Heatwaves without air conditioning or a car

See **survive extreme heat without power** for the core techniques — shading,
night ventilation, wetting the skin, spotting heatstroke. Two things change
when you also have no car:

- **You can't drive to a cooler place**, so the walkable cool space you
  scouted above is doing double duty here — decide where it is before the
  first hot day, not while you're already overheating.
- **An upper-floor flat traps heat worse than a house**, often 5-10°C hotter
  under a sun-facing roof than at street level, and a lift outage turns "go
  downstairs" into a real barrier for anyone frail. Isolation, not heat
  itself, is what has driven the death toll in past European heatwaves — the
  people at risk are usually the ones nobody is checking on, not the ones
  without the fanciest cooling.

## Look out for isolated neighbours

The same isolation applies to people around you, especially older neighbours
on upper floors with no car and no one nearby. A simple check-in agreement —
a knock or a call, twice a day during a heatwave or blackout — costs almost
nothing and catches trouble early; see **prepare for and live through a long
blackout** for the same habit in that context. Use the group-decision
guides in the cooperation category to set this up as a standing block or
building arrangement rather than something improvised each time.
