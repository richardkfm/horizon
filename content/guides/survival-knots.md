# Tie essential knots and lashings

A handful of knots covers most needs: joining ropes, making a loop, securing a
load, and tensioning a shelter. Learn these few well rather than many badly, and
practise until you can tie them in the dark.

> **Rule of thumb:** The best knot is one that holds under load *and* unties
> afterwards. Always leave a "tail" past the knot so it can't slip free.

## The core set

- **Square (reef) knot** — joins two ropes of the *same* thickness for light
  loads (bandages, bundles). "Right over left, left over right." Not for heavy
  loads or for joining different sizes.
- **Sheet bend** — joins two ropes, including *different* thicknesses. More
  reliable than a square knot for towing or extending a line.
- **Bowline** — makes a fixed loop that won't slip or jam, even after heavy
  load. The rescue knot: a loop you can put around a person or a post.
- **Two half hitches** — ties a rope to a post, ring, or tree; quick and secure.
- **Clove hitch** — starts and ends lashings and ties off to a pole; fast to
  adjust.
- **Taut-line hitch** — an adjustable loop that grips under tension; perfect for
  guy lines on a tarp or tent that you need to tighten.

## Lashings (join poles)

- **Square lashing** — binds two poles crossing at right angles, for frames,
  racks, and shelters. Start with a clove hitch, wrap around the crossing 3-4
  times, add "frapping" turns *between* the poles to tighten, finish with a clove
  hitch.
- **Diagonal lashing** — binds poles that cross at an angle and tend to spring
  apart, e.g. braces.

```ascii
                |
                |   vertical pole
          vvvvv | vvvvv
     ===========+===========    horizontal pole
          ^^^^^ | ^^^^^
                |
                |

   v/^  wraps around the crossing, 3-4 turns each way
   +    frapping turns cinched between the poles pull everything tight
```

## Practice and care

- Practise with cord before you need it; muscle memory matters under stress.
- Keep rope dry and coiled; inspect for fraying before trusting it with weight.
- Never put a knotted rope under load near anyone who could be hurt if it fails.
