# Resolve conflict between people fairly

Not every conflict is a decision to be voted on (see the group-decisions
guide) — sometimes it's a dispute between two or three people that's
souring the whole group's mood. Left alone, personal conflict quietly
drains the trust cooperation depends on. Handled well, it can leave a
group stronger than before.

> **Principle:** The goal is repair, not punishment. Look for what each
> person needs to feel safe and respected going forward — not for who was
> "right".

## Do now: de-escalate first

- **Separate people before facts.** If voices are raised or someone feels
  threatened, create space and calm down before trying to resolve anything.
- **Don't triangulate.** Discourage venting to third parties who then carry
  the story back distorted — bring concerns to the person, or to an agreed
  neutral facilitator, not the rumour mill.
- **Name the harm plainly** to whoever caused it, without exaggeration or
  attack: what happened, and what effect it had.

## Bring in a neutral third party

- **Pick someone trusted by both sides**, with no stake in the outcome —
  not the person's manager, ally, or rival.
- **Agree ground rules first:** one person speaks at a time, no
  interrupting, confidentiality unless safety requires otherwise.
- **The facilitator's job is process, not verdict:** they keep the
  conversation fair and moving, they don't decide who's right.

## A simple mediation process

1. **Each side tells their story uninterrupted**, while the other listens
   without responding yet.
2. **Each side restates the other's view** in their own words, until the
   other confirms it's been understood.
3. **Name the need behind the complaint** — safety, respect, being heard,
   fair treatment — rather than staying stuck on the specific incident.
4. **Brainstorm repairs together:** an apology, a changed behaviour, a
   changed arrangement — whatever actually meets the needs just named.
5. **Agree something concrete** — what changes, starting when — and a time
   to check whether it held.

> **Pick this if:** the dispute is between specific people and both are
> willing to talk. Mediation works when there's a relationship worth
> repairing and no immediate danger.

> **Avoid if:** one person is unsafe, unwilling, or using the process to
> re-litigate rather than repair — see "when mediation isn't enough" below
> instead of forcing it.

## When mediation isn't enough

- **Repeated harm, not a one-off** points to a structural problem, not a
  single dispute — bring it to the group's governance process (recall,
  role change) rather than mediating the same conflict again and again.
- **A safety concern** (threats, coercion, exploitation) is not a mediation
  case — protect the person at risk first; this is what recall and boundary-
  setting in the governance guide are for.
- **No obligation to reconcile.** Repair does not mean the people involved
  must keep working closely together — sometimes the fair outcome is
  separating their roles, not forcing continued closeness.
