# Splint fractures and sprains

A broken bone, dislocation, or bad sprain hurts and swells, and the safest first aid
is usually the same: **support the injury, keep it still, and don't try to push a
bone back into place.** Getting the person to proper care comes first.

> **Warning:** First aid only. A clinician should set and check broken bones. Get
> professional help, especially for the head, neck, back, or hip, or if a limb looks
> badly out of shape, is numb, or has lost its pulse or colour.

## Tell what you're dealing with

You often can't be sure it's broken without an X-ray, so **treat a bad injury as a
fracture if in doubt.** Common signs: pain that worsens with movement, swelling,
bruising, deformity, or a grating feeling. For sprains, remember **RICE** (Rest,
Ice, Compression, Elevation).

## Steady the injury

1. **Keep the person still** and calm; don't move them more than necessary.
2. **Support the injured part** in the position you find it — don't force it
   straight or "pop" a joint back.
3. **Stop any bleeding** first with direct pressure (see the bleeding guide), and
   cover open wounds.

## Improvise a splint

A splint stops a broken bone moving while you get help.

1. **Pad a rigid support** — a straight stick, board, rolled mat, or even a folded
   newspaper — along the limb.
2. **Tie it on** above and below the break with cloth strips or cord, firm but not
   so tight it cuts off circulation.
3. **Immobilise the joints** above and below the injury.

```ascii
     [==========================]   <- splint (stick, board, rolled mat)
         ||                ||
     ====##================##====   <- limb, tied above and below the break
         ^^                ^^
       tie here          tie here
        (never directly over the break)
```

*Fig. 1: a splint ties above and below the injury, never over the break itself, to hold it still without adding pressure*

4. For an arm, a **sling** (and binding it to the body) works well; for a leg, you
   can tie the injured leg to the good one as a last resort.
5. **Check fingers/toes** beyond the splint stay warm and pink. If they go pale,
   cold, or numb, loosen the ties.

## Ease swelling and pain

- **Raise** the injured part if it doesn't cause more pain.
- **Cool** with a cloth-wrapped ice pack or cold water for short spells (not
  directly on skin).
- **Rest** it and avoid weight on it until it's been checked.

## Watch for shock

A painful injury can cause shock (pale, cold, clammy, faint). Keep the person warm,
lying down, and reassured while you arrange care.
