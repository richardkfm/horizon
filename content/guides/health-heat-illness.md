# Treat heat exhaustion and heat stroke

Too much heat moves through two stages: **heat exhaustion** (uncomfortable and
unwell but still sweating and alert) and **heat stroke** (the body can no longer
cool itself — a life-threatening emergency). Cooling fast is the whole game.

> **Do now:** Move the person somewhere cool, get them to rest, and cool their skin
> with water and air. If they are confused, drowsy, or stop sweating, treat it as
> heat stroke and get emergency help immediately.

> **Warning:** First aid only. Heat stroke can kill quickly — get professional help
> at once. Cool the person while you wait; don't delay cooling to look for help.

## Heat exhaustion — act early

**Signs:** heavy sweating, tiredness, headache, dizziness, nausea, cramps, fast
breathing, pale clammy skin.

1. **Move to a cool, shaded, or ventilated place** and have them lie down.
2. **Loosen clothing** and cool the skin — cool water on the skin, fanning, cool wet
   cloths on the neck, armpits, and groin.
3. **Sip water** (a little salt or rehydration mix helps if sweating heavily).
4. **Rest** — they should feel better within about 30 minutes. If not, or they get
   worse, treat as heat stroke.

## Heat stroke — emergency

**Signs:** confusion, slurred speech, loss of coordination, very hot skin that may
be dry (sweating has stopped), rapid pulse, seizures, or collapse.

1. **Get emergency help now.**
2. **Cool aggressively** — into shade, strip excess clothing, and douse with cool
   water; fan hard; place wet cloths or ice packs at the neck, armpits, and groin.
   Immersion in cool water is very effective if you can do it safely.
3. **Keep cooling until they improve** or help arrives, and monitor breathing.
4. If unresponsive and not breathing normally, start CPR (see the choking/CPR
   guide).

## Prevent it

Drink before you're thirsty, rest in shade during the hottest hours, and check on
children, older people, and anyone working or ill. See also the *survive extreme
heat* guide.
