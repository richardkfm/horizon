# Find and collect water in the field

You can last only a few days without water, so finding it is a first priority.
Finding it is not the same as making it safe: collect first, then **always test
and treat before drinking** (see the water guides).

> **Important:** Clear-looking water can still carry germs or chemicals. Treat
> all field water unless you are certain it is safe.

## Where to look

- **Downhill and low ground:** valleys, the base of cliffs, and green strips of
  vegetation in dry land often mark water.
- **Follow life:** converging animal tracks, swarming insects, and birds flying
  low at dawn or dusk often lead to water.
- **Rock and soil:** dig at the outside bend of a dry streambed, or where the
  ground stays damp; water may seep in.

## Collect rain and dew

- **Rain** is among the safest water — spread out any clean sheet, tarp, or
  large leaves to funnel it into a container.
- **Dew** — drag a clean cloth through dewy grass at dawn, then wring it out.
- **Plant transpiration** — tie a clear bag over a leafy, non-poisonous branch
  in sun; water condenses inside through the day.

## Make a simple solar still

In damp ground or near greenery, dig a pit, set a container in the centre, cover
the pit with a sheet weighted with a stone over the container, and seal the
edges with soil. Water evaporates, condenses on the sheet, and drips in. Yield
is small but steady.

## Carry and store

- Use any clean sealable container; keep collected water shaded and covered.
- Label treated vs untreated containers so no one drinks the wrong one.

## Then make it safe

Strain out grit through cloth, then **test and treat** — boiling for one minute
is the most reliable method when in doubt.
