# Preserve knowledge through storytelling

For most of human history, everything important was carried in spoken stories,
songs, and sayings — not in writing. When paper and screens fail, storytelling is
how a community keeps its skills, its history, and its sense of who it is.

> **Stories carry values, not just facts.** How you tell of a fair leader or a
> wasted harvest teaches the next generation what the community honours. Tell
> them with care.

## What's worth preserving

- **Practical knowledge:** where the safe water is, which plants heal or harm,
  how to read the seasons, how a task is done step by step.
- **History:** what happened, who was here, the mistakes not to repeat.
- **Values and agreements:** how the group decides, shares, and treats people.

## Make knowledge memorable

The mind remembers stories far better than lists. Use these old tools:

- **Rhyme and rhythm:** facts set to a beat or a tune stick (a planting song, a
  rhyme for the order of first-aid steps).
- **Vivid characters and events:** a memorable person or a striking image anchors
  the lesson.
- **Repetition and refrains:** repeated lines invite the audience to join and
  help everyone remember.
- **Memory walks:** tie facts to places along a familiar path, and "walk" it in
  your mind to recall them in order.

## Pass it on

1. **Tell often** — around fires, during work, at gatherings. Knowledge unspoken
   is knowledge lost.
2. **Let others retell** — the story only survives if more than one person can
   tell it. Have children and newcomers repeat it back.
3. **Keep it honest** — distinguish what is known from what is guessed or
   embellished, especially for survival facts.
4. **Write it down too** where you can — but don't rely on a single fragile copy.
   Spoken and written together is safest.
