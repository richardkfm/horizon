# Heat water with the sun

Heating water is one of the biggest everyday energy uses — and one the sun can do
directly, with no panels, batteries, or electricity. A simple solar water heater
gives you warm water for washing, cleaning, and comfort from sunlight alone.

> **Mind the scald and the freeze.** Solar-heated water can get hotter than you
> expect — test before use, especially around children. In cold climates, drain
> or protect the system so it can't freeze and burst.

## How it works

Sunlight warms a dark surface; that heat passes into water. Two simple
approaches:

- **Batch heater (simplest):** a dark tank or barrel in a sunny, sheltered spot,
  ideally in an insulated box with a glass or clear lid to trap heat. The whole
  tank warms through the day.
- **Collector + tank:** water flows through dark pipes or a panel in the sun and
  rises (when hot) into a storage tank above — a **thermosiphon** that needs no
  pump.

## Build a basic batch heater

1. **Paint a metal tank or barrel matt black** so it soaks up heat.
2. **Box and insulate it:** set it in a box lined with insulation, with the sunny
   side covered by glass or clear plastic to trap warmth (a mini greenhouse).
3. **Face the sun:** point the glazed side toward the midday sun and tilt it for
   your latitude and season.
4. **Plumb it:** cold water in at the bottom, hot water drawn from the top.
   Insulate the pipes so heat isn't lost on the way to the tap.

## Build a thermosiphon collector

- Run **dark pipe** (or a salvaged radiator) in an insulated, glazed box facing
  the sun — the **collector**.
- Place the **storage tank above** the collector.
- Connect so cold water leaves the tank bottom to the collector, and hot water
  rises back to the tank top. Heat drives the flow; no pump needed.

## Get the most from it

- **Insulate the tank and pipes well** so the day's heat lasts into the evening.
- **Use it when it's hottest** (afternoon/evening) and size the tank to a day's
  hot water.
- **Maintain it:** keep glazing clean, check for leaks, and in winter protect
  against frost (drain it, or use a freeze-tolerant design).
