# Treat bites, stings, and wildlife encounters

Most bites and stings are painful rather than dangerous, but a few need fast,
specific action — and knowing which is which matters more than any single
remedy.

> **Do now:** Swelling of the face, lips, or throat, difficulty breathing, or
> widespread hives after a sting or bite is a severe allergic reaction
> (anaphylaxis). Use an epinephrine auto-injector if the person has one, and
> treat this as life-threatening — get emergency help immediately.

## Insect stings

1. **Remove a bee stinger** by scraping it out sideways with a fingernail or
   card — don't pinch it, which squeezes more venom in.
2. **Cool the area** with a cold compress to ease pain and swelling.
3. **Watch for a spreading allergic reaction** for the next 30 minutes, even
   if the person hasn't reacted to a sting before.

## Tick bites

- **Remove a tick promptly** with fine tweezers, gripping as close to the
  skin as possible, and pull straight out with steady, even pressure — don't
  twist, jerk, or crush the body.
- **Clean the bite site** with soap and water or antiseptic afterward.
- **Watch the site for weeks afterward** — an expanding rash, fever, or
  joint pain in the days or weeks after a bite can mean a tick-borne illness
  and needs medical attention.
- **Prevent bites**: tuck trousers into socks in tall grass or woodland,
  wear long sleeves, and check the whole body (especially hairlines,
  behind ears/knees) after time outdoors — the sooner a tick is removed,
  the lower the risk of disease.

## Snake bites

> **Risk:** Do not cut the wound, try to suck out venom, apply ice, or use a
> tight tourniquet — all of these outdated methods cause more harm than the
> bite itself and can cost a limb.

1. **Keep the person calm and still** — movement speeds venom through the
   body faster than staying still does.
2. **Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing** near the bite before
   swelling starts.
3. **Keep the bitten limb at or slightly below heart level** and get to
   medical care as fast as safely possible.
4. **Note the snake's colour and shape** from a safe distance if you can, but
   never risk a second bite trying to catch or kill it.

## Animal bites

- **Wash the wound thoroughly** with soap and running water for several
  minutes — this alone cuts infection and disease risk substantially.
- **Control any bleeding** with direct pressure (see stopping severe
  bleeding).
- **Treat any bite from a wild or unknown animal as a possible rabies risk**
  and seek medical care — rabies is close to always fatal once symptoms
  start, but is preventable with prompt treatment.

## Avoid the encounter in the first place

- **Store food well away from where you sleep** and never in a tent — see
  choosing and setting up a campsite.
- **Make noise while moving through thick brush** so animals have time to
  move away before you're close.
- **Never approach, corner, or feed wildlife** — an animal that isn't afraid
  of people is more dangerous to the next person, not less.
- **Keep well clear of young animals** — a parent is usually close by and
  will defend them.
