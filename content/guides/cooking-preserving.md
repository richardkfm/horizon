# Preserve food by drying, fermenting, and pickling

A good harvest is wasted if it rots before you eat it. Preserving lets a glut feed
you through lean months with no refrigeration. The three most useful, low-tech
methods are drying, fermenting, and pickling — all simple, all plant-based.

> **Cleanliness and the right conditions keep you safe.** Spoilage and harmful
> microbes are the risk. Work clean, follow the conditions each method needs
> (dryness, salt, acid), and when in doubt — off smell, mould, slime, fizzing
> where there shouldn't be — throw it out.

## Drying (simplest)

Removing water stops most spoilage. Great for fruit, beans, herbs, and many
vegetables.

- **Sun/air dry:** slice thin and spread on racks in warm, dry, airy shade or
  gentle sun; protect from insects with cloth or netting.
- **Solar dryer:** a simple glazed box that heats and vents air dries faster and
  cleaner.
- **Done when** pieces are leathery to brittle with no moisture left. **Store
  fully dry** in sealed containers in the dark; check for any damp or mould.

## Fermenting (lacto-fermentation)

Salt plus the vegetables' own good bacteria creates acid that preserves and adds
nutrition (think sauerkraut, kimchi-style ferments, pickles):

1. **Salt the vegetables** (shredded cabbage, etc.) and pack them tight.
2. **Keep them under the brine** — submerged, away from air — using a weight. Air
   is where mould grows.
3. **Leave at room temperature** to bubble and sour over days to weeks; taste
   until you like it, then move somewhere cool to slow it.

## Pickling (in vinegar)

Submerging food in a strong acid (vinegar) stops spoilage and keeps it crisp:

- Pack clean vegetables in clean jars and cover completely with vinegar (often
  with salt, and spices).
- Keep everything submerged and sealed; store cool and dark.

## Other keepers

- **Cool, dark storage** (a cellar, clamp, or cool room) keeps roots, squash,
  onions, and apples for months as they are.
- **Jams and fruit leathers** preserve fruit with the fruit's own sugars and
  drying.

## Build a store

Label everything with what and when, use oldest first, and keep checking your
store for spoilage. A full, well-managed larder is real food security through
winter or hard times.
